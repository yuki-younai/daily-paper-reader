## Abstract 

Reinforcement learning (RL) has become a cen-tral post-training paradigm for large language models (LLMs), but its performance is highly sen-sitive to the quality of training problems. This sensitivity stems from the non-stationarity of RL: rollouts are generated by an evolving pol-icy, and learning is shaped by exploration and reward feedback, unlike supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with fixed trajectories. As a result, prior work often relies on manual curation or simple heuristic filters (e.g., accuracy), which can ad-mit incorrect or low-utility problems. We pro-pose GradAlign , a gradient-aligned data selec-tion method for LLM reinforcement learning that uses a small, trusted validation set to prioritize training problems whose policy gradients align with validation gradients, yielding an adaptive curriculum. We evaluate GradAlign across three challenging data regimes: unreliable reward sig-nals, distribution imbalance, and low-utility train-ing corpus, showing that GradAlign consistently outperforms existing baselines, underscoring the importance of directional gradient signals in nav-igating non-stationary policy optimization and yielding more stable training and improved fi-nal performance. We release our implementa-tion at https://github.com/StigLidu/ GradAlign .

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Data Selection for LLMs 

Data selection has emerged as a critical lever for enhancing the efficiency and performance of LLMs (Koh & Liang, 2017). Early approaches largely relied on static heuristics to filter low-quality web text, utilizing metrics such as per-plexity thresholds, language identification, deduplication, and rule-based filters (Penedo et al., 2023; Raffel et al., 2019; Abbas et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2024). Beyond filtering, optimizing the mix-ture of data sources has been standard practice to balance general capabilities with specific domain knowledge (Xie et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2024). More recent strategies em-ploy diversity-based sampling (Zhang et al., 2024) or proxy models to score data quality (Wettig et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024). However, these methods predominantly rely on hu-man intuition or general-purpose heuristics rather than a principled evaluation of how specific data points contribute to the model’s learning trajectory, often leading to subopti-mal alignment with downstream tasks (Xia et al., 2024). 

2.2. Influence Estimation and Gradient Alignment 

To transcend heuristic selection, researchers have adopted Influence Functions (Koh et al., 2019; Grosse et al., 2023) to theoretically quantify the impact of a training instance on validation loss. In the context of LLMs, gradient-based methods have been successfully adapted for data selec-tion tasks during pre-training and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Engstrom et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). Specifi-cally, gradient alignment techniques—which select training samples whose gradient updates maximize the cosine simi-larity with a trusted validation set—have proven effective for instruction tuning (Zhang et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2025a). Despite these advances, the application of gradient-based selection is largely con-fined to imitation learning paradigms (SFT). The efficacy of such influence estimation in RL, where the model learns from self-generated rollouts and sparse rewards rather than ground-truth targets, remains significantly understudied. 

2.3. LLM Reinforcement Learning 

Reinforcement Learning (RL) is increasingly central to un-locking advanced LLM capabilities, particularly for com-plex reasoning (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025) and autonomous agentic tasks (Du et al., 2025). While standard Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) remains a cornerstone, recent advances such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024) and the reasoning-focused DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) demonstrate that RL can substantially out-perform supervised baselines. However, RL algorithms are notoriously sensitive to data quality and reward noise; for example, training on trivial or unsolvable problems can in-duce instability or reward hacking (Hu et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2023). As a result, although considerable effort has been devoted to selecting valuable data using heuristic signals such as gradients or parame-ter distributions (Li et al., 2025b;a; Liang et al., 2025), the curriculum of which problems to present to the agent, and 

when , is typically treated as static. This overlooks the po-tential of dynamic, quality-aware data selection to stabilize and accelerate RL training. In this work, we propose gra-dient alignment as a principled approach for dynamically selecting high-quality data during the RL process.

The learner selects a subset of these problems for training, aiming to filter out low-quality candidates and maximize performance on a downstream evaluation task. ## 4. GradAlign 

We propose GradAlign , an online data selection method for reinforcement learning. In realistic RL settings, training data is abundant but highly heterogeneous, and reward signals can be noisy or uninformative to learning. Accuracy-based filtering methods are commonly used in prior work (Yu et al., 2025), based on the intuition that LLMs learn most from problems that are neither too easy nor too hard and lie near the decision boundary. However, accuracy alone does not distinguish which problems induce beneficial policy updates. GradAlign addresses this limitation by selecting high-quality samples through gradient alignment. 3GradAlign: Gradient-Aligned Data Selection for LLM Reinforcement Learning 

Our key insight is that a training problem is valuable only if updating the policy on it improves performance on a trust-worthy validation set representing the downstream task. We approximate this improvement using first-order policy gra-dients: GradAlign treats the average policy gradient on the validation set as a target direction and ranks candidate prob-lems by their alignment with this direction. As illustrated in Figure 1, GradAlign operates iteratively by estimating validation gradients, selecting aligned training problems, and updating the policy, while resampling rollouts in each round to adapt to the evolving policy. 

4.1. Validation Improvement as a Proxy 

Unlike LLM fine-tuning, where detailed solutions are pro-vided in training data, in RL settings, we only observe re-ward signals. This makes it difficult to estimate the utility of individual training problems directly. We therefore use 

validation improvement as a proxy for problem utility: a training problem is useful if updating the policy on it im-proves performance on the validation set Pv , which is more likely to transfer to the held-out test set. A direct approach would be to perform RL updates on each candidate prob-lem and measure the resulting change on Pv , but this is computationally prohibitive.

4.4. On-Policy Resampling 

Because the policy evolves during RL training, both the expected validation accuracy and the validation GRPO gra-dients change over time. To mitigate the bias introduced by stale rollouts in advantage estimation, we resample all validation problems and recompute the average validation gradient at the beginning of each data-selection round. This design differs from Zhu et al. (2025), which estimates gradients using offline rollouts with importance sampling. Although importance sampling can be unbiased, as policy evolves, newly emerging behaviors may not be covered by the offline rollout distribution, preventing reliable estimation of their gradients. Moreover, importance sampling typically incurs high variance when the behavior and target policies diverge, requiring substantially more samples for stable estimation and increasing computational cost.

Low-Utility Training Data As RL training scales up, practitioners increasingly rely on large web-mined datasets (Yue et al., 2024; Du et al., 2025). Unlike dis-tribution imbalance, where useful data is underrepresented, the challenge here is that many training problems are on-distribution but overly simple, yielding valid rewards while providing limited learning signal for the target task. GradAlign addresses this setting by prioritizing problems whose gradients align with validation performance and downweighting low-utility samples. 

5.2. Computational Overhead 

GradAlign introduces additional computations for estimat-ing gradient alignment, but the overhead is modest for three reasons. First, the validation set is small, so computing the target gradient incurs a negligible cost relative to policy training. Second, gradient computation for training can-didates adds only a constant-factor overhead compared to accuracy-based filtering (Kaplan et al., 2020). Finally, by prioritizing more informative training problems, GradAlign improves sample efficiency, reducing the number of inef-fective updates and leading to better final performance than training on the full dataset. 

## 6. Experiments 

In this section, we evaluate the effectiveness of GradAlign across diverse scenarios. Through these experiments, we aim to verify the capability of GradAlign to select high-quality training data targeting specific domains from a large and noisy training dataset. 

6.1. Experimental Setup RL Framework All experiments are conducted using GRPO (Shao et al., 2024). We use a sparse binary reward that indicates whether the final answer is correct, and all re-wards are provided using Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct as a judge. Detailed RL training hyperparameters can be found in Ap-pendix A. 

Scenario 1: Distinguishability Test with Noisy Rewards 

To rigorously assess the selector’s capacity to discriminate valid learning signals from stochastic noise, we establish a controlled experiment involving systematic perturbation of the training set. Utilizing the DAPO training corpus (Yu et al., 2025), we introduce controlled corruptions to 50% of the samples, whereby rewards are decoupled from model outputs and instead sampled from a Bernoulli distribution (p = 0 .5). This procedure generates a subset of data that sta-tistically mimics the difficulty of “complex” problems (50% pass rate) while devoid of helpful gradient information. 

Scenario 2: Unbalanced Dataset To evaluate alignment capabilities under distributional shift, we construct a het-erogeneous dataset comprising 4k Countdown Game prob-lems (Gandhi et al., 2024), 20k WebInstruct problems, and 10k DAPO problems (Yu et al., 2025). Given that the target domain (Countdown Game) constitutes merely ≈10% of the aggregate pool, this setting challenges methods to retrieve domain-specific knowledge from imbalanced distributions. We employ a selection ratio of q = 20 (selecting the top 5%), with validation and test sets consisting exclusively of Countdown Game problems. 

Scenario 3: Low-Utility Training Data In this setting, we utilize the WebInstruct-filtered-unverified dataset (Ma et al., 2025) as the training set, which is a large-scale col-lection (808k problems) gathered from internet sources that represents typical noisy, uncurated training data. The down-stream tasks and validation/test splits are specified as fol-lows: • MMLU-Pro: We train Qwen2.5-1.5B-Math-Instruct on WebInstruct. The target domain is the Math cat-egory of MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024), which is 6GradAlign: Gradient-Aligned Data Selection for LLM Reinforcement Learning 

partitioned into validation and test sets. This setting evaluates GradAlign’s ability to identify training prob-lems that induce policy updates beneficial to multiple-choice mathematical reasoning. • SuperGPQA & TheoremQA: We investigate scenar-ios where the training distribution is broad but the target domain is narrow. Specifically, we train Qwen3-8B-Base on WebInstruct . To improve computational efficiency, we apply a preliminary difficulty filter (pass rates ∈ [0 .2, 0.8] ) before RL selection. We utilize Su-perGPQA (Team et al., 2025) and TheoremQA (Chen et al., 2023) as the downstream target domains. • Mathematical Reasoning Task: We train Qwen3-8B-Base on the same pre-filtered WebInstruct sub-set described above. Data selection is guided by AMC22 (Mathematical Association of America, 2022) as the validation set, while performance is evaluated on AMC23 and AIME24/25. This setting tests whether GradAlign can leverage a small, competition-level val-idation set to extract similarly challenging reasoning problems from a broad and noisy training distribution. 

6.2. Baselines 

We compare GradAlign with three data selection strategies: 1. Random Selection: Uniformly samples training data. Serves as a lower bound. 2. Accuracy Greedy (AccGreedy): Prioritizes samples with pass rates closest to 50%, based on the heuristic that problems near the decision boundary provide the strongest signal (Sun et al., 2025c). 3. LearnAlign (Align) (Li et al., 2025a): A method that selects data based on gradient similarity within the training set without using external validation guidance.

## 8. Conclusion 

We presented GradAlign, a gradient-aligned data selection approach for LLM reinforcement learning that uses a small validation set to identify training problems whose policy-gradient updates most consistently improve downstream per-formance. Across multiple challenging regimes, including noisy rewards, distribution imbalance, and low-utility web data, GradAlign produces more stable training and stronger final results than heuristic and prior gradient-similarity base-lines, suggesting policy gradient direction as a reliable signal for the attribution of RL data and the construction of the curriculum, which can be broadly used to improve the effi-ciency of training downstream tasks in LLM reinforcement learning.
