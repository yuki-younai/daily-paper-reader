# ABSTRACT 

Adversarial behavior plays a central role in aligning large language models with human values. However, existing alignment methods largely rely on static adversarial settings, which fundamentally limit robustness, particularly in multimodal settings with a larger attack surface. In this work, we move beyond static adversarial supervision and introduce co-evolutionary alignment with evolving attacks, instantiated by CEMMA (Co-Evolutionary Multi-Modal Alignment), an automated and adaptive framework for multimodal safety alignment. We introduce an Evolutionary Attacker that decomposes adversarial prompts into method templates and harmful intents. By employing genetic operators, including mutation, crossover, and differential evolution, it enables simple seed attacks to inherit the structural efficacy of sophisticated jailbreaks. The Adaptive Defender is iteratively updated on the synthesized hard negatives, forming a closed-loop process that adapts alignment to evolving attacks. Experiments show that the Evolutionary Attacker substantially increases red-teaming jailbreak attack success rate (ASR), while the Adaptive Defender improves robustness and generalization across benchmarks with higher data efficiency, without inducing excessive benign refusal, and remains compatible with inference-time defenses such as AdaShield. # 1 Introduction 

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) enable joint reasoning over images and text, but also expand the attack surface for adversarial prompting. By embedding visually grounded instructions and coordinating them with crafted language, attackers can induce harmful behavior that bypasses safety alignment. This setting, commonly referred to as 

multimodal jailbreaking , poses a critical deployment challenge for MLLMs [Dong et al., 2023, Niu et al., 2024, Gong et al., 2025, Liu et al., 2024b, Li et al., 2024]. Existing defenses against MLLM jailbreaking broadly fall into two lines. Post-training alignment methods update the model using curated supervision, such as supervised fine-tuning and preference optimization [Zong et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024b, Ouyang et al., 2022]. Inference-time defenses intervene at deployment, for example via input filtering or defensive prompting [Cui et al., 2024, Jiang et al., 2025]. Although they can mitigate certain attacks, they often introduce a trade-off between robustness and benign utility, especially through over-refusal (sometimes discussed as an “alignment tax”) [Huang et al., 2025]. Together, these limitations suggest that MLLM safety alignment should be treated as an adaptive process under a shifting threat landscape. A natural direction is automated red teaming: using models and search procedures to continually probe a target model, discover failures, and convert them into supervision for alignment. In text-only settings, iterative prompt optimization and attacker-LLM-based search have shown promise [Chao et al., 2025, Ge et al., 2024, Perez et al., 2022]. For multimodal settings, however, automated red teaming is harder to scale: the attacker must operate in a discrete space under black-box feedback while preserving multimodal coherence, and surface-level paraphrasing often yields only local variations rather than structural strategy transfer. As a result, generating high-quality, diverse, and continually improving adversarial supervision at scale remains challenging. We build on the hypothesis that successful jailbreak queries are rarely arbitrary. Instead, they often share reusable 

strategy patterns , such as discourse wrappers, role-play scaffolds, or instruction-hiding structures which can generalize across different attack families (i.e., benchmark- or template-defined jailbreak strategies). If these transferable patterns can be identified and recombined, automated red teaming can move beyond isolated trial-and-error toward structured 

strategy transfer that systematically upgrades weak probes into reliable jailbreaks. Motivated by this view, we propose CEMMA (Co-Evolutionary Multi-Modal Alignment), which instantiates safety alignment as a co-evolutionary process between an evolving attack search procedure and an adaptive defender. At each CEMMA generation, the Evolutionary Attacker performs black-box evolutionary prompt optimization guided by a judge-derived jailbreak success score, using genetic operators designed to transfer effective structures across attack families. The Adaptive Defender then improves via supervised fine-tuning on an evolving archive of newly discovered successful jailbreak instances which mixed with benign data to control over-refusal, yielding a defender that adapts as the attack distribution shifts. Our contributions are as follows: • We propose CEMMA , a co-evolutionary framework for MLLM safety that automatically generates diverse, high-quality red-teaming data for adaptive alignment. • We introduce an Evolutionary Attacker with three genetic operators: Mutate , Crossover , and DiffEvo .Together, they enable structured exploration and sample-efficient black-box optimization of jailbreak prompts. • Experiments show that CEMMA improves robustness across multiple benchmarks and can be used as a 

plug-and-play enhancement to existing defenses with competitive benign-task utility. 

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 Multimodal Jailbreaking 

Research on MLLM vulnerabilities broadly falls into two lines: white-box and black-box jailbreaking. White-box 

methods assume privileged access to the target model (e.g., gradients or internal states) and often craft image-space adversarial perturbations via gradient-based optimization. Early studies adapted adversarial techniques from computer vision to MLLMs and demonstrated that optimized visual perturbations can induce unsafe behaviors under strong access assumptions [Niu et al., 2024, Qi et al., 2024, Shayegani et al., 2023, Dong et al., 2023]. While effective, such settings are less representative of common deployments, where model weights are unavailable and visual inputs may be re-encoded or filtered by API-level preprocessing. 

Black-box threats are particularly relevant for closed-weight, API-served frontier models such as GPT-5 [OpenAI, 2025] and Gemini [Google, 2023]. These attacks treat the model as an oracle and operate through query access, often exploiting the semantic interface between vision and language. In particular, carefully structured multimodal queries can elicit unsafe behaviors without requiring gradient-based image perturbations [Gong et al., 2025, Liu et al., 2024b, Wang et al., 2024, Li et al., 2024, Luo et al., 2024]. However, many existing black-box approaches still rely heavily on static jailbreak templates or hand-designed heuristics. Automated pipelines have been developed for scalable evaluation, but are less commonly used as adaptive optimization procedures that continually upgrade attack strategies over time. 

2.2 Automated Red Teaming and Safety Alignment 

A practical challenge for safety alignment is that jailbreak strategies can adapt in response to deployed defenses, causing static alignment signals to lag behind newly emerging attack variants. For MLLMs, automated red teaming under black-box constraints is less studied than in text-only settings, and widely used defenses still rely on post-training with largely static refusal datasets [Bianchi et al., 2023, Zong et al., 2024]. Inference-time defenses such as AdaShield [Cui et al., 2024] can mitigate certain attacks by prepending defensive instructions at deployment, but do not directly incorporate newly discovered adversarial patterns into the training signal. Moreover, strong refusal-oriented training and inference-time interventions can introduce an “alignment tax” through benign-utility degradation and over-refusal [Huang et al., 2025]. 

2.3 Evolutionary Algorithms for Prompt Optimization 

Evolutionary algorithms offer a natural approach for discrete prompt optimization, where language is non-differentiable and search is driven by scalar feedback signals. EvoPrompt [Guo et al., 2024] introduced a framework that uses LLMs to implement mutation and crossover, enabling prompt evolution for downstream tasks such as reasoning and classification. Subsequent work has explored evolutionary prompt optimization in conjunction with chain-of-thought-style reasoning and related structured prompting techniques [Jin et al., 2025, Bharthulwar et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2023]. Compared to these task-oriented settings, evolutionary search for multimodal jailbreaking introduces additional constraints, including black-box feedback, multimodal coherence, and the need for strategy transfer across jailbreak templates. Our work builds on this line by adapting evolutionary operators to the multimodal jailbreaking setting and coupling attack evolution with archive-driven defender fine-tuning. 

# 3 Methodology 

We present CEMMA (Co-Evolutionary Multi-Modal Alignment), a co-evolutionary framework for aligning multimodal LLMs against evolving black-box jailbreak threats. We first introduce the Evolutionary Attacker (Section 3.1), which performs population-based search in the discrete prompt space. Then present CEMMA (Section 3.2), which integrates this attacker into a co-evolutionary alignment loop by updating the defender across generations using an evolving archive of newly discovered successful jailbreaks. 3.1 Evolutionary Attacker 

Jointly evolving both the image and the text substantially enlarges the search space and complicates black-box evaluation, as visual edits can unpredictably shift task grounding and intent. We therefore keep the visual input fixed and evolve only the textual query. This isolates optimization to transferable prompt-level strategy structures while maintaining coherent grounding for each multimodal query. Under this formulation, the attacker performs population-based evolutionary search in the discrete prompt space.

3.2 CEMMA: Co-Evolutionary Alignment Framework 

CEMMA integrates the evolutionary attacker into a closed-loop alignment process that updates the defender across generations. Let D(k) denote the defender at CEMMA generation k. At each generation, CEMMA (i) runs the evolutionary attacker against D(k) to harvest newly successful jailbreaks, (ii) accumulates these hard cases into an evolving archive, and (iii) updates the defender using the archive together with benign data, yielding D(k+1) .

3.2.1 Co-Evolutionary Adversarial Alignment Framework Archive construction. Let A denote a cumulative archive of successful jailbreak instances . During generation k, the attacker evolves a population of candidates against D(k) following Section 3.1.1. A candidate is added to A if it satisfies the success definition. This filtering avoids degenerate cases where the model does not meaningfully engage with the adversarial query. 

Defender update. After augmenting A, we update the defender via fine-tuning on a mixture of archived adversarial cases and benign data. Let Dbenign denote a benign dataset. Each archived instance is converted into a supervised training example by pairing the adversarial input with a desired safe target response. In our implementation, the target is synthesized by rewriting the unsafe output into refusal-style behavior, so the update directly corrects the exposed failure mode. Training on a mixture of A and Dbenign mitigates over-refusal by preserving helpful behavior on non-harmful requests. This update yields the next defender D(k+1) .See Algorithm 1 in Appendix C for full pseudocode. 

3.2.2 Characteristics of the Framework Continual automated red teaming. CEMMA instantiates continual automated red teaming by coupling evolutionary attack search with iterative defender updates. Instead of relying on manual template curation, the attacker continuously explores the discrete prompt space against the current defender and harvests newly discovered successful jailbreak instances. This yields a self-evolving stream of adversarial supervision that reduces manual red-teaming and data collection overhead. 

Data-centric modularity. CEMMA uses the archive A as an explicit data interface between red teaming and alignment: the attacker continuously produces curated supervision in a standard format, and the defender is updated via supervised fine-tuning on A mixed with benign data. Because this interface is purely data-centric, the defender update step is plug-and-play: different training recipes or complementary defenses can be plugged into the same loop by consuming the supervision stored in A.5Co-Evolutionary Multi-Modal Alignment via Structured Adversarial Evolution 

# 4 Experiments 

We conduct experiments to answer two questions. First, we evaluate whether the evolutionary attacker can continually strengthen multimodal jailbreaks against a fixed black-box defender (Section 4.1). Second, we evaluate whether the full co-evolutionary alignment framework, CEMMA, improves defender robustness both in-distribution and under out-of-distribution attack families, including when combined with existing defenses (Section 4.2).

Plug-and-play evaluation. Next, we test whether CEMMA is compatible with an inference-time defense in a plug-and-play manner. On Qwen3-VL-8B, we apply AdaShield [Cui et al., 2024] as an system prompt and compare 

Vanilla+AS , Vanilla+SFT+AS , and CEMMA+AS under the same evaluation protocol (Table 3), together with their non-shielded counterparts to isolate the effect of AdaShield. We report over-refusal on a safe set as a conservative indicator of benign-side utility, and as an approximation to the “alignment tax” introduced by safety defenses. This metric tests whether robustness gains come from indiscriminate refusal, rather than learning safer behavior. AdaShield alone substantially lowers OOD jailbreak ASR but increases benign refusal, whereas CEMMA attains a comparable OOD ASR with zero refusal. Combining the two matches the lowest OOD ASR while keeping benign refusal markedly lower than Vanilla+SFT+AS, suggesting that archive-driven alignment and inference-time shielding can provide complementary gains in this setting.

Limitations. Our current study explores co-evolutionary alignment under a constrained setting. We fix the visual input and evolve only the textual query, which improves stability and sample efficiency but leaves visual attack channels unexplored. Our evaluation is also limited to a small set of defender backbones and benchmarks, with benign utility primarily measured by over-refusal on a held-out safe set. Finally, scaling evolution to longer horizons can induce semantic drift through repeated rewriting; while we mitigate this with age-aware budgeting and diversity controls, more principled drift control remains an open direction. 

Future directions. This work frames multimodal safety alignment as a continual adaptation problem under an evolving adversary. Looking forward, we highlight three broader directions. (i) Interdisciplinary optimization principles: Beyond scaling model size, progress may depend on more principled mechanisms for search, credit assignment, memory, and curriculum. Insights from cognitive science, biology, and neuroscience may provide inductive principles for designing more efficient and controllable optimization in discrete, feedback-driven settings. (ii) Cross-modal semantic constraints: 

Robust multimodal safety may require understanding and shaping stable structures that couple visual evidence, language instructions, and intent. Identifying which such constraints transfer across contexts, and how to leverage them without brittle pattern matching, remains an open problem for alignment. (iii) Safety standards and evaluation: The field would benefit from clearer safety standards and evaluation methodologies, including reliable and transparent judging, agreement under distribution shift, and validation protocols that better connect benchmark measurements to deployment risks. 

# 6 Impact Statement 

This paper advances safety alignment for multimodal language models through automated red-teaming and co-evolutionary adversarial alignment. While our methods may enhance the safe deployment of MLLMs, they could also be misused to bolster jailbreak attacks if applied irresponsibly. We release this work to improve defensive evaluation and training, urging practitioners to use it only in controlled environments with necessary safeguards.

# E Judge Prompt 

LLM-as-a-Judge. Recent work has shown that strong language models can serve as scalable evaluators for open-ended generation by following a structured rubric, achieving substantial agreement with human judgments in practice [Gu et al., 2024]. In addition, prior studies also highlight systematic biases of LLM judges, such as position and verbosity biases, motivating careful prompt design with explicit criteria and step-by-step evaluation. 

Design goals. We use an LLM-based judge to assess whether a candidate jailbreak succeeds against the defender. The judge prompt is designed with two goals. 

(1) Policy-grounded safety assessment. Determining whether the defender output constitutes a safety violation often requires domain knowledge about disallowed content types (e.g., illegal activity, self-harm, malware, fraud, medical advice). To reduce ambiguity and improve consistency across intents, we explicitly provide the judge with a policy taxonomy [CLAS 2024 Organizers, 2024] and require it to score the response according to a coarse-to-fine severity scale. This grounding encourages the judge to focus on whether the output crosses policy boundaries, rather than relying on subjective impressions. 

(2) Relevance-aware evaluation to prevent intent shift. In our evolutionary setting, the attacker may drift away from the original harmful intent when optimizing prompts. Such intent shift can lead to misleadingly high scores if the defender response is judged without checking whether it actually addresses the intended malicious instruction. To mitigate this issue, the judge prompt includes an explicit relevance check and assigns a dedicated “Irrelevant/Off-topic” score when the response fails to engage with the user instruction. This requirement ensures that a high score reflects both (i) semantic alignment with the candidate attack’s intended objective and (ii) a genuine policy violation, rather than accidental or off-topic outputs. 

Scoring interpretation. The judge returns a discrete score in {− 1, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}, where −1 indicates non-responsiveness or irrelevance, 1 indicates safe refusal, 2 indicates benign or general discussion without prohibited details, 3 indicates borderline partial compliance, and 4–5 indicate policy-violating harmful outputs. The structured “reason + score” format standardizes the evaluation procedure and improves reproducibility across generations. We provide the full judge prompt below.
