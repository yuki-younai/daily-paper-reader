## Abstract 

Large language model (LLM) alignment relies on complex reward signals that often obscure the spe-cific behaviors being incentivized, creating crit-ical risks of misalignment and reward hacking. Existing interpretation methods typically rely on pre-defined rubrics, risking the omission of "un-known unknowns", or fail to identify objectives that comprehensively cover and are causal to the model behavior. To address these limitations, we introduce Obj-Disco, a framework that automat-ically decomposes an alignment reward signal into a sparse, weighted combination of human-interpretable natural language objectives. Our approach utilizes an iterative greedy algorithm to analyze behavioral changes across training check-points, identifying and validating candidate ob-jectives that best explain the residual reward sig-nal. Extensive evaluations across diverse tasks, model sizes, and alignment algorithms demon-strate the framework’s robustness. Experiments with popular open-source reward models show that the framework consistently captures > 90% of reward behavior, a finding further corroborated by human evaluation. Additionally, a case study on alignment with an open-source reward model reveals that Obj-Disco can successfully identify latent misaligned incentives that emerge alongside intended behaviors. Our work provides a crucial tool for uncovering the implicit objectives in LLM alignment, paving the way for more transparent and safer AI development.

Second, even assuming comprehensive data oversight, hu-mans face a significant cognitive bottleneck in enumerating objectives to attain coverage. Humans generally lack the capacity to track the subtle distributional shifts required to identify "unknown unknowns" across thousands of outputs (Ribeiro & Lundberg, 2022; Gao et al., 2023; Dunlap et al., 2024; 2025; Zhong et al., 2025; Williams et al., 2025). Even when a potential objective is identified, validating its se-mantic meaning requires grounding it in concrete examples; without a curated set of illustrative trajectories, develop-ers may struggle to interpret how an abstract objective like "helpfulness" actually materializes across different contexts. Consequently, manually deriving a full set of valid objec-tives becomes practically infeasible to scale up (Zhong et al., 2022). To address these scalability challenges, recent research has introduced automated frameworks to help understand model behavior. However, these systems operate primarily as benchmarks constrained by predefined metrics. Unlike discovery-based approaches, the majority of these works are prescriptive and evaluate models using a fixed set of objec-tives (Pan et al., 2023; Murthy et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025; Ren et al., 2024; Ryan et al., 2025). This inherently limits their ability to uncover unanticipated "unknown unknowns." Conversely, descriptive “proposer-validator” frameworks attempt to explain differences between datasets or models from a blank slate (Dunlap et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2022; 2025; Dunlap et al., 2025). However, they generally oper-ate in static settings, comparing fixed snapshots rather than analyzing the continuous dynamics of the training process (Ryan et al., 2025). Consequently, prior works fail to lever-age a rich source of signal regarding what LLMs actually learn during alignment: the trajectory of model weights checkpointed during training. Without this temporal infor-mation, these methods struggle to disentangle the model’s pre-existing inductive biases from the specific behaviors ac-tively reinforced by the reward signal. Our work bridges this gap by leveraging these checkpoints to facilitate higher-fidelity objective discovery without relying on predefined categories. 

We introduce Obj-Disco, a framework that automatically decomposes an LLM’s implicit alignment objectives into a set of objectives expressed using natural language, which we refer to as the Discovered Interpretable Reward (DIR) .Our approach rests on the core assumption that the reward signal driving alignment can be approximated as a function of separate basis functions, or objectives, as also pointed out by Silver et al (2021) (Jang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025; Go et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2025). Accordingly, we frame this task as a sparse representation problem and employ an iterative, greedy algorithm inspired by match-ing pursuit to sequentially identify the objectives that best explain the residual reward signal (Mallat & Zhang, 1993). To ensure both coverage and validity, each iteration of Obj-Disco consists of a structured Objectives Discovery phase followed by an Objectives Verification phase. During discov-ery, we address the intractability of the exponential objective space by using a proposer LLM to generate candidates based on response trajectories observed across multiple training checkpoints. By dynamically sampling inputs where the residual error is largest, we explicitly guide the proposer to target unexplained behavioral shifts and select the candidate that minimizes this residual. Subsequently, the verifica-tion phase utilizes LLM-as-a-Judge evaluations to screen the selected candidate against formal criteria, ensuring it is both human-interpretable and follows a predictable trend throughout alignment fine-tuning. Finally, to aid developer understanding, Obj-Disco provides Objective Explanations ,a curated set of sample trajectories selected to maximize both trend salience and semantic diversity, offering concrete evidence for each discovered objective. We validate Obj-Disco through an extensive experimental suite spanning multiple alignment algorithms, model sizes, and tasks. In controlled settings with known ground-truth objectives, our framework recovers the target signals with high fidelity, consistently modeling over 90% of the true alignment behavior. Experiments on popular open-source reward models further demonstrate that Obj-Disco can ap-proximate complex, implicit reward functions with similar accuracy. To verify the practical utility of these discoveries, we conduct human-subject studies which confirm that our DIRs are significantly more causal to the final policy behav-ior than those identified by baseline methods. Participants also rated our Objective Explanations as having higher fi-delity to the underlying objective signals. Finally, a targeted case study on safety alignment reveals that Obj-Disco con-sistently successfully surfaces latent misaligned incentives, such as increased permissiveness in discussing illegal acts, which other baselines rarely detect.

Given a sequence of models πθ1 , . . . , π θT from an align-ment process on a target dataset D, our main goal is to uncover the Discovered Interpretable Reward (DIR), a set of interpretable objectives ˆR that explains the underlying reward signal R∗. 3.3. Exemplar-Based Objective Explanations 

To help users understand the behavior of a discovered ob-jective rn, Obj-Disco presents explanations in the form of a small set of input-response trajectory samples illustrative of that objective, which we refer to as Objective Explanations (or OEs). As manual inspection of the entire dataset would be cognitively prohibitive, our goal is to select a sparse subset of points that maintains as much explanatory utility as possible. We achieve this by leveraging the notion of 

diminishing returns : the marginal utility of adding a sample decreases if similar samples are already represented in the set. This notion is formally encapsulated by the property of submodularity , which allows us to design optimization algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees (Nemhauser et al., 1978) (Appendix A.3.3). ## 4. Experimental Setup 

We comprehensively validate the efficacy of Obj-Disco by first assessing reconstruction fidelity with a controlled syn-thetic environment where ground-truth objectives are explic-itly known. Second, we demonstrate real-world applicability by decomposing the implicit signals of popular open-source reward models, including DeBERTaV3 and the Skywork Reward family, across varied policy models and alignment algorithms. To rigorously verify our findings, we conduct two user studies evaluating the causal impact of our dis-covered rewards and the fidelity of the generated Objective Explanations. Finally, we perform extensive ablations to iso-late the contributions of key components of our algorithm. 

4.1. Setup Details 

We evaluate on two open-source LLMs of different families: Llama-3.1-8B (meta llama) and Qwen3-4B (Team, 2025). We perform alignment using two different RLHF algorithms: 

PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and GRPO (Shao et al., 2024). 

Tasks: We evaluate on four standard open-source datasets across distinct domains. Summarization: Reddit TL;DR dataset (TLDR) from Stiennon et al. (2022), a popular benchmark for summarizing long posts into a concise “Too Long; Didn’t Read” format. Multi-Turn Dialogue: An-thropic Helpful and Harmless (HH-RLHF) dataset (Bai et al., 2022), which contains conversational dialogues in-volving anything from astrophysics to relationship advice. 

Instruction-Following: 10k prompt instances from GPT4-Alpaca, which uses GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) to gen-erate output answers using the same prompt instances as the Stanford Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023). Diverse Domains (Math/Coding/Safety): Skywork-Reward dataset (Sky), which consists of 80k prompts from a diverse array of domains such as math, coding, safety, information seeking, and data analysis (Liu et al., 2024). 

Baseline Methods . To the best of our knowledge, no meth-ods exist yet for our proposed problem setting. As a result, we compared Obj-Disco against the following baseline meth-ods: (1) Iter-Filter , an iterative, filtering-based approach, and (2) Zero-Shot , a standard zero-shot approach. Iter-Filter 

is inspired by the algorithm from VibeCheck (Dunlap et al., 2025) and IterAlign (Chen et al., 2024). We generate a set of objectives at once, filter out the ones which do not satisfy the two criteria, and then iteratively do that until we obtain k

objectives. Zero-Shot serves to represent a simple discovery-based baseline; obtain a random sample and hypothesize a set of k objectives based on their trajectories. More details about the baselines in Appendix A.4.

## 5. Experimental Results 

5.1. Controlled And Open-Source Reward Models 

We construct a controlled alignment process where the ground-truth reward signal, R∗, is defined as a linear combi-nation of a pre-defined set of three LLM-as-a-Judge natural language objectives. This allows us to directly measure how faithfully Obj-Disco can recover these objectives. The spe-cific objectives used are directly inspired by objectives used in OpenAI (2024); Bai et al. (2022); Glaese et al. (2022), are tailored to each task, and are detailed in Appendix A.5.2. We also perform experiments using open-source reward models (RMs), notably reward-model-deberta-v3-large-v2 

(DeRM) and Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.2-3B (SkyRM). For both, we vary across Llama-8B and Qwen-4B for the policy model. We use HH-RLHF and TLDR for DeRM and Skywork preference dataset for SkyRM (Liu et al., 2024). For Alpaca, we trained our own RM on a single dimension of a multi-objective preference dataset (Jang et al., 2023). Results are shown in Figure 3. Open-source reward model qualitative results are shown in Figure 2. In all experimental settings, Obj-Disco was the only method to consistently surpass a Model-Fit of 90%, while often achieving higher scores than both Iter-Filter and Zero-Shot . Zero-Shot some-times approaches the performance of Obj-Disco, albeit at a notably higher variance. One notable limitation of Zero-Shot 

is how it’s limited by the context-length or capabilities of the proposer LLM, which may be consequential in resource-constrained settings. We qualitatively illustrate the effects of this in Section 5.2. Additional results in Appendix A.5. 

5.2. Case Study: Detecting Latent Misalignment 

To demonstrate Obj-Disco’s utility in safety auditing, we analyze a real-world scenario prone to unintended behav-ioral shifts: multi-turn dialogue aligned towards helpful-ness. To do this, we used GRPO with the open-source gpt2-large-helpful-reward-model . Although designed to promote general helpfulness, this reward model can inadvertently in-centivize negative behaviors, i.e. sycophancy. We compare the discovered objectives of four baselines: (1) Iter-Filter ,(2) Zero-Shot , (3) a Fixed-α variant restricted to a set of 

α pre-defined objectives, testing the value of open-ended search; and (4) Limited-Zero-Shot , which uses a restricted context window to simulate resource-constrained environ-ments where analyzing large trajectory sets is infeasible. The resulting objectives are presented in Figure 4. Notably, Obj-Disco was the only framework to consistently surface latent misaligned behaviors, such as “increase permissive-ness in discussing illegal or unethical acts" , in 3 out of 4 trials. The baselines surfaced misalignment in, at most, 1 out of 4 trials. This result underscores the fragility of Zero-Shot 

methods and highlights the robustness of our residual-driven selection strategy for uncovering subtle safety risks. Further details are provided in Appendix A.4.1 and A.5.1. 

5.3. Objective Explanations Human-Subject Evaluation 

We conducted a user study to validate the utility of our Ob-jective Explanations (OEs) component for human end-users. Our central hypothesis posits that OEs provide representa-tive trajectories that allow users to accurately identify the underlying objective. Participants were presented with sam-ple response trajectories generated by either Obj-Disco or a random baseline and tasked with selecting the correct objec-tive from a set of four possible ones (including distractors). We report the accuracy of identifying the true objective: Obj-Disco achieved an accuracy of 39.9% ± 6.5% , significantly outperforming the random baseline (25.5% ± 5.8%). Aone-tailed binomial test against chance (25%) confirms the statistical significance of our method ( p < 0.001 ), whereas the baseline showed no significant deviation ( p = 0 .462 ), demonstrating the superior explanatory power of our ap-proach. Further details are provided in Appendix A.4.2. 

5.4. Objectives Causality Human-Subject Evaluation 

We conducted a user study to validate the core capabil-ity of Obj-Disco: identifying the implicit objectives that causally drive policy behavior. Our goal was to determine if a policy model trained on our Discovered Interpretable Reward (DIR) exhibits behaviors indistinguishable from those of the original aligned model. Participants were pre-sented with a target response generated by the final check-point of the original aligned model and were asked to se-lect the most behaviorally similar response from models aligned with the DIRs from Obj-Disco and baseline meth-ods. Therefore, a higher selection frequency for Obj-Disco would indicate that its DIRs more accurately capture the causal drivers of the original aligned behavior. We report selection frequencies: Obj-Disco ( 35.6% ± 4.3% ), Iter-Filter (16.7% ± 3.3%), Zero-Shot (27.1% ± 4.0%), and base model (20.6% ± 3.6%). A chi-square test rejected uni-form selection ( p < 0.001 ). Bonferroni-corrected pairwise comparisons confirmed that Obj-Disco was preferred over 

Iter-Filter (p < 0.001 ), Zero-Shot (p < 0.010 ), and the base model ( p < 0.001 ), indicating that Obj-Disco’s discovered objectives better reproduce the causal behavioral patterns of alignment. 5.5. Ablation: Importance of Model Trajectory 

A major differentiator of Obj-Disco from related works is that we leverage a trajectory of multiple model checkpoints for discovering objectives which are causal to the behaviors. We attempt to illustrate the importance of leveraging the full trajectory here. To do so, we obtain both quantitative and qualitative results for a variation of Obj-Disco which only uses the base model and final model checkpoint for discovery, which we refer to as Obj-Disco-Static. We obtain quantitative results using the controlled evaluation setting: HH-RLHF, GRPO, Llama-8B. The results are shown in Figure 5. As can be seen, the Obj-Disco-Static ablation often results in a lower Model-Fit score without the richer information from the trajectory. We also evaluate this within the misalignment case study scenario due to its real world applicability We show the qualitative results in Figure 6. As can be seen, the DIRs from Obj-Disco-Static capture critical misaligned behaviors in only one out of four trials. Obj-Disco, on the other hand, does so in three out of four trials. Additionally, the DIRs from Obj-Disco-Static seem to often be correlated, or simi-lar, to each other, resulting in decreased variety. Collectively, these findings suggest that the intermediate checkpoint tra-jectory provides a richer signal than what can be captured by static endpoints alone. 1 2 3 4

## 6. Related Works 

LLM Evaluation. More recent research is specifically auditing the alignment of LLMs. Some works audit for value alignment by analyzing model responses on interac-tive benchmarks and specialized datasets (Ye et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025; Murthy et al., 2025; Hendrycks et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2025; Ren et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025a; Pan et al., 2023). In a study on the feasibility of auditing, Marks et al. first induced a hidden objective into a model and then tasked separate teams with discovering it using interpretability and prompting tools (2025). While insight-ful, these approaches typically rely on evaluating models against a pre-defined set of values or find a single, known hidden objective. Our work does not make such assump-tions as developers often cannot anticipate the full range of behaviors that may emerge during alignment. 

Understanding LLM Misalignment. A significant body of research focuses on understanding misalignment (Betley et al., 2025; Entezami & Naseh, 2025; Liang et al., 2023). Many studies have cataloged such unintended consequences, such as producing overly long responses (Singhal et al., 2024), reducing output diversity (Kirk et al., 2024), degrad-ing refusal behavior (Song et al., 2025), and misleading humans or giving malicious advice (Wen et al., 2024; Bet-ley et al., 2025; Ghandeharioun et al., 2024; Tamkin et al., 2023; MacDiarmid et al., 2025). Other works have pro-posed to mitigate certain misalignment through techniques like causal reward modeling (Wang et al., 2025), balanced preference datasets (Singhal et al., 2024), or decoupling reward from confounding variables (Shen et al., 2023). Our work instead focuses on discovery from scratch rather than mitigation or documentation of a bias. 

Reward Model Interpretability. Jiang et al. leverage controlled perturbations to interpret RMs and propose to generate contrastive explanations by modifying responses along a predefined set of attributes (e.g., helpfulness, ver-bosity) and observing the impact on the RM’s preference (2025b). Christian et al. perform an exhaustive search over the RM’s vocabulary, identifying tokens that are considered most "optimal" and "pessimal" to reveal any inherent biases (2025). Others have focused on decomposing a scalar re-ward into a multi-objective vector, making the trade-offs more explicit (Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). Fi-nally, Marks et al. applied sparse autoencoders to interpret implicit RMs by analyzing features obtained from the activa-tions between the base and fine-tuned model (2023). Instead of analyzing predefined, potentially correlated attributes, we discover objectives from scratch and aim to isolate out the directional nature of alignment and maximize coverage. 

Additional related works in Appendix A.1 .

## 7. Conclusion 

We introduced Obj-Disco, a framework which decomposes an opaque alignment reward signal into a set of interpretable natural language objectives. To do so, we employ an itera-tive greedy algorithm that analyzes the model’s behavioral trajectory, sequentially identifying and verifying the ob-9Discovering Implicit Large Language Model Alignment Objectives 

jectives that best explain the residual reward signal. Our extensive empirical evaluations confirm the method’s effi-cacy: in controlled settings with known objectives and with popular open-source reward models, Obj-Disco consistently recovers over 90% of the reward behavior, a finding strongly corroborated by human user studies and a case study identi-fying latent misalignment. Primary limitations include com-putational cost and reliance on LLM-based judges, which may introduce inherent biases (Zheng et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2025) (limitations in Appendix A.7). We believe Obj-Disco to be a step toward scalable, yet transparent, AI alignment. Future work may include extending this framework into an online and real-time setting. 

## Impact Statement 

This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field of Machine Learning. There are many potential societal consequences of our work which may result from better understanding the behavior of the policy model after RL fine-tuning. We hope that our work may pave the path for safer and more transparent AI deployments by making more transparent any potential AI misalignment, biases, or other harmful behaviors.

## A. Appendix 

A.1. Additional Related Works LLM Evaluation. Evaluating the capabilities and behaviors of large language models (LLMs) has been a major focus of the natural language processing (NLP) community. Early efforts sought to improve upon classic metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) by developing more nuanced criteria for open-domain generation, such as fluency and conciseness (Pang et al., 2020; Sellam et al., 2020; Banerjee & Lavie, 2005). This has evolved into comprehensive benchmarking suites that measure model performance across a diverse set of pre-defined tasks and metrics (Liang et al., 2023; Srivastava et al., 2023; Kiela et al., 2021). To assess qualities like helpfulness and clarity without a ground-truth, others have leveraged LLMs themselves as scalable judges of model outputs (Mehri & Eskenazi, 2020a; Li et al., 2019; Mehri & Eskenazi, 2020b). 

Automated Data Analysis. Automating the discovery of patterns in unstructured data is a long-standing goal, with classical methods like topic models aiming to extract topical variations from documents (Blei, 2012). More recently, a prominent line of work has leveraged LLMs to automatically propose and validate interpretable, natural language descriptions of differences between two static sets of data, such as texts or images (Zhong et al., 2022; Dunlap et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2023; 2025; Torii et al., 2024; Byun et al., 2023; Kahng et al., 2024). This includes systems like VibeCheck, which discovers qualitative differences to explain pairwise model preferences (Dunlap et al., 2025). More recently, StringSight is a tool using similar approaches to automate our understanding of agentic AI systems by analyzing their outputs (Dunlap, 2025). Report Cards is another work proposing to use LLMs to automatically generate human-interpretable, natural language summaries of language models, in the form of report cards (Yang et al., 2024). Ryan et al. focuses on the related task of interpreting human feedback with a set of correlated metrics (2025) but assumes a predefined initial set of metrics and a static feedback dataset. Our work is inspired by this paradigm of automated, language-based data analysis but makes several crucial departures. First, rather than comparing two (or more) distinct models or static datasets, we analyze the temporal evolution of a single model across its alignment trajectory. Second, our method does not rely on pairwise preference data or an initial set of predefined metrics as required by VibeCheck and AutoMetrics (Dunlap et al., 2025; Ryan et al., 2025); instead, it operates directly on the underlying reward signal. Finally, our framework generalizes beyond discovering binary predicates (e.g. (Zhong et al., 2025)) by aiming to identify any valid objective that exhibits a predictable trend, and we introduce a novel, principled sampling strategy based on alignment residuals to guide the discovery process. 

Interpretable LLM Alignment Frameworks. While many alignment methods treat an LLM’s values as an implicit byproduct of training data, a distinct paradigm focuses on codifying desired behaviors into explicit, interpretable documents (Ouyang et al., 2022). These approaches, often called “Model Specifications” or “Constitutions”, provide a transparent foundation for alignment. Prominent examples include OpenAI’s Model Spec (OpenAI, 2024), Anthropic’s Constitutional AI (CAI) (Bai et al., 2022), and DeepMind’s Sparrow, which used a set of 23 natural-language rules (Glaese et al., 2022). This concept has been extended to democratically-sourced constitutions (Huang et al., 2024) and multi-objective alignment techniques that integrate a transparent set of goals into the model (Go et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Some work, like IterAlign (Chen et al., 2024), even aims to automatically discover a constitution from model behavior to improve alignment. Our work complements these prescriptive approaches; rather than defining a constitution for the model to follow, we aim to discover the implicit objectives a model has already learned from its alignment process. 

Mechanistic Interpretability. Mechanistic interpretability is a bottom-up approach to interpretability which aims to study the fundamental components of models through fine-grained analysis of layers, neurons, and connections. The goal is often to uncover causal relationships transforming inputs to outputs, essential for fostering trust and transparency. Elhage et al. developed a mathematical framework to analyze up to a two layer attention-only transformer (2021). Foote et al. and Bills et al. proposed to leverage a larger language model to predict what a neuron in the smaller model represents (2023; 2023). Rather than study neurons as the fundamental unit, Black et al. proposed to study polytopes as the fundamental building block instead (2022). Bereska & Gavves provide a comprehensive survey on mechanistic interpretability approaches for AI safety (2024). Rather than study the internal mechanisms of the language models, our proposed method directly uses the model output behavioral changes to discover and quantify a set of interpretable objectives. 

Exemplar-Based Explanations. One paradigm for enhancing the transparency of complex machine learning models is the use of exemplar-based explanations, which ground model decisions in specific, representative data instances rather than abstract feature scores (Wei et al., 2025; Hoang-Xuan et al., 2024). Concretely, prototype-based methods, originally developed for computer vision, aim to improve explainability by comparing inputs with representative examples rather than using abstract feature weights (Chen et al., 2019). For LLMs, Wei et al. developed a prototype-based model to explain 18 Discovering Implicit Large Language Model Alignment Objectives 

the predictions of an LLM in a post-hoc manner (2025). Purohit et al. investigated into exemplar selection methods for improving LLM reasoning (2024). As users often desire for the sets of exemplars to be manageable in size, other works have applied submodular optimization to select such subsets, leveraging the property of diminishing returns to balance representativeness and semantic diversity (Tschiatschek et al., 2014; Sinha et al., 2011). Our work extends this paradigm by applying it to the temporal dimension of LLM alignment, using trajectory-based exemplars to explain the directional behavioral shifts actively reinforced during the alignment process. 

A.2. Problem Definition 

If the output scores for the rewards are normalized to be in [0, 1], as detailed in Section 2, then the square root in Equation 7 acts to push higher (or make worse) the Obj-Error scores for a worse set of ˆR.In practice, to ensure a rigorous evaluation and to avoid overfitting, we calculate Obj-Error using a chronological train-test split across the model checkpoints. We designate the first set of models, from checkpoint 1 to Tsplit , as the training set, and the subsequent models as the test set. The function C is fit exclusively on the training set; we learn its parameters by regressing the scores of our discovered objectives against the ground-truth reward scores for the early-stage model responses. We then evaluate the final Obj-Error on the held-out test set of later-stage model responses. This chronological split ensures we are measuring how well the objective relationships learned early in alignment generalize to the model’s behavior trajectory. A lower Obj-Error on this test set indicates a better and more generalizable fit. When linear regression is used as the composition function C, we enforce positive coefficients as our discovery method assumes increasing trends across the checkpoints. Note that this does not reduce the generalization of our method as any objective can be reframed from decreasing to increasing with its semantic opposite.

Trend-Predictability Verification. To operationalize the trend-predictability criterion, we define Ftrend as a library of parametric functions widely established in the learning curve literature for modeling performance trajectories (Viering & Loog, 2022). We specifically include models that capture distinct optimization dynamics, ranging from heavy-tailed improvement to rapid saturation: 1. Linear Growth: f (t) = at + b. Represents strictly increasing, constant-rate improvement. 2. Logarithmic Growth: f (t) = a ln( t) + b. Models improvement that is rapid initially but decays over time without a finite ceiling. 3. Power Law with Asymptote: f (t) = c − at −b. Frequently observed in LLM scaling laws, this models "heavy tail" learning that approaches an asymptotic limit c as t → ∞ .4. Exponential Saturation: f (t) = S + g(1 − e−k(t−1) ). Models rapid initial learning that quickly converges to a performance ceiling, where S is the starting score, g is the total possible gain, and k is the convergence rate. We emphasize that while we employ these specific forms to capture standard learning behaviors, Obj-Disco is theoretically agnostic to the contents of Ftrend and can accommodate any user-defined class of trend functions. A.6. Additional Discussion 

A.6.1. H OW TO DECIDE THE NUMBER OF OBJECTIVES ?In practical settings, the optimal number of objectives k is often unknown a priori. We recommend treating k as a tunable parameter determined through an "elbow method" approach, similar to selecting the number of components in Principal Component Analysis. Specifically, developers should run Obj-Disco iteratively, monitoring the marginal decrease in Obj-Error on the validation set at each step. We suggest stopping the discovery process when the marginal improvement in the reconstruction error falls below a predefined threshold or when the newly discovered objective is semantically redundant with the existing set. This data-driven approach ensures that the discovered set ˆR is sparse yet sufficiently expressive to capture the core alignment dynamics. 24 Discovering Implicit Large Language Model Alignment Objectives 

What Happens If We Discover More Objectives? We show qualitative results of this scenario in Figure 34. As shown in Figure 34, objectives 1-3 initially match very closely with those in the ground-truth set (hidden to Obj-Disco). The final two objectives may be seen as related, or potentially more ”narrow”, objectives which Obj-Error then tends to discover during the latter stages as it attempts to minimize the residuals. 

A.7. Limitations 

Our work presents a promising approach, but it has several practical limitations. First, Obj-Disco is computationally intensive, incurring some time and financial costs (due to the LLM-as-a-Judge setup) to discover the set of objectives ˆR. In order for Obj-Disco to be feasible, our proposed method requires relatively inexpensive models, such as GPT-4o-mini to serve as the proposer and scoring models. However, such judges are also subject to inaccuracies in judgment and potential biases in their outputs, potentially skewing the results (Zheng et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2025). Furthermore, the generative nature of our objective proposer introduces stochasticity. Different runs may therefore produce varied sets of objectives, a challenge common to automated discovery methods that makes exact replication difficult (Dunlap et al., 2025). Finally, the quality of the discovered objectives may be sensitive to hyperparameter selection. For instance, the thresholds for our human-interpretability and trend-predictability criteria, ϵinterp and ϵtrend , may require careful tuning to ensure stable and meaningful results. 25 Discovering Implicit Large Language Model Alignment Objectives
