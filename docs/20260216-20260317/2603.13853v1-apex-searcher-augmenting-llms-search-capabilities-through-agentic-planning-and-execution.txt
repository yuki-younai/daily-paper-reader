## Abstract 

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), grounded in large language models (LLMs), serves as a vital approach in acquiring and leverag-ing external knowledge across various domain applications. When confronted with complex multi-hop questions, a single round of information retrieval is often insufficient for accurate reasoning and problem resolution. Thus, most of the existing research focuses on enhancing search capabilities by increasing the number of re-trieval iterations or refining query formulations. However, these approaches often pay less attention to LLMs’ task planning abilities, which are of critical importance in effectively managing complex retrieval processes. To address this gap, this paper proposes a novel 

Agentic Planning and EX ploration framework, denoted as APEX-Searcher, to augment LLMs’ searching capabilities. Specifically, we start by employing reinforcement learning with task decomposi-tion rewards to enhance the LLM’s agentic planning ability. Build-ing upon the resulting plans, we then implement a multi-round retrieval-reasoning paradigm, utilizing supervised fine-tuning to further optimize the LLM’s exploration performance. Extensive ex-periments demonstrate that our proposed framework has achieved significant improvements in task decomposition, planning, and multi-hop RAG performances across multiple benchmarks.

## 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable ability to process, generate, and comprehend human language across a vast spectrum of applications [ 55 ]. They are trained on massive text corpora, have demonstrated emergent capabilities in reasoning, summarization, and few-shot learning, fundamentally altering the landscape of natural language processing [ 26 ]. However, the power of these models is intrinsically tied to the knowledge they encode within their parameters during the pre-training phase[ 30 ]. This reliance on static, parametric knowledge introduces two critical vulnerabilities. First, the model’s world knowledge is frozen at the time of training, rendering it incapable of accessing information be-yond its "cut-off date" and unable to incorporate real-time or rapidly evolving facts [ 15 , 21 ]. Second, when confronted with queries that fall outside their training distribution or require precise, verifiable facts, LLMs are prone to generating plausible but factually incorrect information—a phenomenon widely termed "hallucination" [ 3 , 10 ]. Recently, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as the canonical framework for connecting LLMs to external, non-parametric knowledge sources [ 21 ]. The RAG architecture elegantly combines a pre-trained language model with an information re-trieval system, having proven highly effective at mitigating hal-lucinations and providing access to up-to-date information (e.g., Wikipedia or a proprietary database) for a wide range of knowledge-intensive tasks [ 6]. However, The standard RAG is largely confined to tasks where the necessary information can be located within a single retrieval pass, and it reaches a distinct breaking point when faced with multi-hop questions—complex queries [ 25 ] that necessitate the synthesis of information from multiple, often inter-dependent, pieces of evidence to derive a final answer [9, 39, 49]. To address the limitations of single-pass retrieval, the research community has largely relied on iterative RAG as the approach for multi-hop question answering [ 20 , 22 , 29 , 40 , 46 , 50 ]. In this paradigm, the model engages in multiple rounds of retrieval and

Typically, the output from one iteration, such as an intermediate answer or a generated thought, is used to formulate a new query for the subsequent retrieval step, creating a chain of information gathering. Inspired by these works, some other studies have explored reinforcement learning (RL) based on iterative retrieval [ 2, 13 , 35 , 36 , 43 , 53 , 56 ], aiming to train LLMs to combine reasoning and searching through RL without using any supervised data on reasoning steps. Despite iterative RAG represents a symptomatic fix rather than a fundamental solution. The core issue is that these approaches, while increasing the quantity of retrieval, often fail to improve the quality of the overarching search strategy. As this paper argues, much of the existing research overlooks the critical importance of an LLM’s intrinsic task planning abilities in resolving complex retrieval tasks. Without a global reasoning structure to guide the search, these methods remain vulnerable to the same failure modes that plague standard RAG, including disruption by irrelevant docu-ments, inefficient search paths characterized by over-planning or repetitive queries, and a lack of robust control over the reasoning trajectory [ 11 ]. This reveals a crucial distinction between tacti-cal execution and strategic planning . Iterative RAG methods operate at a tactical level. At each step, the model reactively asks, 

"Based on the information I just found, what should I look for next?" 

[ 29 , 50 ]. In contrast, strategic approach would first ask, "To answer this complex question, what is the overall plan? What are the distinct sub-problems I must solve, and in what logical order?" This proactive, planning process provides a global vision and robust scaffold for the subsequent retrieval steps. In this paper, we introduce APEX-Searcher , a novel framework that augments LLMs’ search capability through decomposing and planning complex search tasks via RL and augments LLMs’ multi-round retrieval exploration capability via SFT. The central idea of our work is that enhancing an LLM’s intrinsic ability to formulate a coherent search plan is more effective for multi-hop question answering than solely relying on increasing the number of retrieval iterations. Specifically, the key innovation of Apex-Searcher is a task-oriented training framework structured around a hierarchical optimization process: High-Level Task Planning and Low-Level Multi-Round Retrieval . High-Level Task Planning: Initially, the LLM is trained as a planning agent. Its policy is optimized to de-compose a complex, multi-hop query into a logical and executable sequence of simpler, solvable sub-questions. The reward signal is based on the utility of the generated plan for the downstream re-trieval task. Low-Level Multi-Round Retrieval: Subsequently, the LLM is trained as an execution agent. Given a plan (i.e., a sequence of sub-questions), its policy is optimized to execute this plan by per-forming effective multi-round retrieval to find the correct answer for each sub-question. The primary contributions of this paper are summarized as fol-lows: 

• We propose a novel RAG framework, denoted as APEX-Searcher, focusing on augmentint the LLM’s retrieval and reasoning ca-pabilities through Agentic Planning and EX ploration. 

• For complex multi-hop questions, we adopt RL using task de-composition rewards to improve the LLM’s agentic planning abilities. 

• We construct a planning-based retrieval paradigm and signifi-cantly enhance the LLM’s exploration capability during multi-turn retrieval via supervised fine-tuning. 

• Extensive experimental results on multiple challenging multi-hop QA benchmarks verify the effectiveness of our proposed APEX-Searcher framework. 

## 2 Related Works 2.1 Classical Retrieval-Augmented Generation 

Traditional RAG, often referred to as "Naive RAG" in recent liter-ature [ 6], emerged as a foundational paradigm to address critical limitations of LLMs, such as hallucinations [ 3, 10 ], outdated knowl-edge, and knowledge-intensive tasks [ 15 ]. Rooted in the integration of external knowledge bases with LLMs, this framework follows a three-stage pipeline: indexing, retrieval, and generation [ 16 , 21 ]. Early implementations of traditional RAG focused on improving knowledge-intensive tasks such as open-domain question answer-ing [ 52 ]. For instance, [ 21 ] demonstrated that RAG outperforms vanilla LLMs on ODQA benchmarks (e.g., Natural Questions [ 18 ], TriviaQA [ 14 ]) by leveraging Wikipedia as an external knowledge source. Similarly, Dense Passage Retrieval [ 16 ] introduced dense retrievers to improve retrieval precision, laying the foundation for subsequent RAG advances. Despite its success, traditional RAG faces notable limitations: retrieval often suffers from low preci-sion/recall (e.g., retrieving irrelevant or redundant chunks), genera-tion may produce hallucinations inconsistent with retrieved context [6 , 34 , 51 ]. These shortcomings motivated the development of more advanced RAG variants. 

## 2.2 Iterative Retrieval-Augmented Generation 

Iterative Retrieval-Augmented Generation was proposed to over-come the limitations of traditional RAG’s one-time retrieval, which often fails to provide sufficient context for complex, multi-step rea-soning tasks [ 32 ]. Iterative RAG adopts a cyclic pipeline: it repeat-edly retrieves information from external knowledge bases based on the initial query and intermediate generation outputs, enabling the model to accumulate incremental context and refine its under-standing of the task [ 1 , 5, 19 , 32 , 40 , 48 ]. Key contributions to Itera-tive RAG include frameworks that synergize retrieval and genera-tion to enhance mutual performance. For example, ITER-RETGEN [ 32 ] introduces a "retrieval-enhanced generation" and "generation-enhanced retrieval" loop to ensure that each new chunk aligns with the evolving task context. IRCoT [ 40 ] is a new multi-step QA ap-proach that uses chains of thought (CoT) to guide the retrieval and takes advantage of the retrieved results to improve CoT. Evaluations on benchmarks like HotpotQA [ 49 ] (multi-hop QA) have shown that Iterative RAG outperforms traditional RAG by capturing nuanced, multi-step dependencies in information. How-ever, challenges persist, including potential semantic discontinuity across iterations and accumulation of irrelevant information, which have spurred research into adaptive stopping criteria (e.g., confi-dence thresholds) to balance retrieval depth and efficiency [12]. ## 2.3 Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation 

Recent surveys define an agentic LLM as a system that can au-tonomously reason, act, and interact with its environment to achieve a goal [ 28 ]. Unlike passive models that simply respond to prompts, an agentic system can decide when and how to use external tools (like a search engine or a retrieval engine), and adapt its strategy based on feedback [ 22 , 29 , 40 , 46 , 50 ]. This paradigm shift is mo-tivated by the need to address complex, long-horizon tasks that require more than a single inference pass. For example, Search-o1 [ 46 ] framework extends the agentic RAG mechanism by incor-porating a Reason-in-Documents module. Building on this foun-dation, some agentic RAG work has focused on improving the model’s reasoning ability during the search process through RL [ 2, 13 , 35 , 36 , 43 , 53 , 56 ]. For example, Search-R1 [ 13 ] optimizes LLM reasoning paths through multi-round search interactions and achieves stable RL training with the help of retrieval token masking. 

## 2.4 Planning in Agentic Systems 

While the agentic RAG mentioned in Section 2.3 has achieved re-markable performance, none of them have considered the agent’s planning ability as a crucial factor for improving retrieval accu-racy. Planning is a fundamental stage for an agent before executing retrieval, and it is an important step in breaking down complex problems into sub-problems [ 45 , 54 ]. To enhance the planning abil-ity of agents, a significant amount of research has utilized explicit or implicit structured knowledge to guide the planning process during the reasoning stage [ 4, 7 , 8 , 17 , 24 , 42 ]. Many works have also fo-cused on making planning ability a learning objective for the agent, enabling it to optimize its decision-making process through search, feedback, or large-scale training [ 27 , 37 , 47 ]. However, few of these works have conducted research on the combination and training of planning and complex multi-round retrieval in the execution and retrieval phase after planning. 

## 3 Methodology 

In this section, we present APEX-Searcher ( Agentic Planning & 

EX ploration Searcher ), a novel framework engineered to solve complex, multi-hop questions as shown in Figure 1. Our method-ology is built upon a structured RAG pipeline, which is opera-tionalized through a specialized agent architecture. This architec-ture systematically decouples the intricate reasoning process into two distinct, synergistic phases: (1) RL-based Agentic Planning for strategic question decomposition and (2) SFT-based Agentic Exploration for iterative, multi-hop information retrieval.

## 3.3 RL-based Agentic Planning

We frame this decompo-sition task as a sequential decision-making problem and employ RL to train a"Planning Agent." This agent, implemented as a large lan-guage model, learns an optimal policy, 𝜋 𝑝𝑙𝑎𝑛 for generating logical and efficient reasoning plans.

## 3.4 SFT-based Agentic Exploration 

To enhance the model’s performance in multi-round retrieval, we constructed a batch of multi-round fine-tuning datasets which have undergone accuracy screening consistent with the reasoning of APEX-Searcher through Self-instruct [ 41 ]. These datasets are fur-ther trained on the Execution Agent via SFT, enabling the model to learn how to accurately solve problems in accordance with this set of reasoning paradigms. The data generation pipeline involved the following steps: 

Seed Task Collect: We sampled and constructed a set of ques-tion instructions from the multi-hop task data training sets 2Wiki-MultiHopQA [9], HotpotQA [49], and MuSiQue [39]. 

Instruction Generation: We asked the advanced model such as Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct [ 38 ] and Deepseek-v3 [ 23 ] to generate instructions in accordance with the aforementioned multi-round reasoning paradigm based on task planning, and generated a batch of multi-round instruction data as shown in Figure 3. 

Filtering and Validation: The generated data underwent a rigorous automated filtering process to ensure its quality and align-ment with our framework’s logic. We discarded instances that were trivial, contained flawed reasoning, or produced factually incorrect answers based on the simulated retrieved context. This step was crucial for preventing the model from learning erroneous patterns. Through this process, we sampled data from the 2WikiMulti-HopQA, HotpotQA, and MuSiQue training sets to construct a final fine-tuning dataset of 14,604 high-quality, multi-turn retrieval in-struction instances. By fine-tuning on this specialized dataset, we effectively teach the Execution Agent to internalize the complex, iterative logic of the APEX-Searcher framework. This training enables the model to learn not just what information to retrieve, but how to strate-gically explore a knowledge base, manage context across multiple turns, and synthesize information to accurately solve problems in accordance with our defined reasoning paradigm. ## 4.4 Ablation Study 

To demonstrate the effectiveness of RL-based Agentic Planning and SFT-based Agentic Exploration, we conducted ablation experiments on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct. We tested the differences in the model’s performance on various benchmarks with different combinations of method components (including whether to adopt Planning, whether to use RL to train the Planning process, and whether to use SFT to train the Exploration process). It can be seen from Table 2 that our training of the model on both Agentic Planning and Agentic Exploration is effective and necessary. Both contribute to the improvement of the final performance of Apex-Searcher. The specific conclusions of the ablation study are as follows: 

• Synergy of Components: The model achieves optimal per-formance across both scales when all components are simul-taneously enabled. Specifically, the 7B model improves from a baseline of 27.55 to 37.64, representing an increase of approxi-mately 36.6%. The 3B model advances from a baseline of 13.42 to 33.45, reflecting a substantial improvement of 149%. 

• Fundamental Role and Limitations of the Planning Mod-ule: Introducing the "Plan" module in isolation, without RL optimization or SFT, yields limited performance gains and may even negatively impact smaller models. When only the Plan module is enabled, the Eval score is 30.39 (an improvement over the 27.55 baseline). However, for the smaller model, the Eval score drops to 12.63, falling below the baseline of 13.42. This indicates that merely requiring the model to perform com-plex task decomposition (Planning) increases task difficulty. For the less capable 3B model, the absence of targeted training to guide plan execution results in forced decomposition, which introduces noise or leads to error accumulation. 

• Effect of planning RL: Incorporating RL on top of the plan-ning module significantly enhances the model’s ability to solve complex problems. A comparison between "Plan only" and "Plan + RL" reveals the following: the 7B model improves from 30.39 to 33.82, and the 3B model improves from 12.63 to 18.10. This demonstrates that RL optimization effectively improves the quality of plan generation. 

• Effect of exploration SFT: SFT is critical for enhancing re-trieval and reasoning capabilities on specific sub-problems, par-ticularly for smaller models. In the absence of planning, enabling only Exp SFT causes the 3B model’s Eval score to surge from 13.42 to 23.52. This suggests that SFT significantly compensates for the foundational deficiencies of smaller models. By enabling the model to better utilize tools or corpora for reasoning, SFT serves as a key factor in improving "execution capability."

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we focus on the limitations of existing RAG frame-works in handling complex, multi-hop questions. We introduced APEX-Searcher, a novel framework that enhances an LLM’s search capability by explicitly separating strategic planning from iterative execution. Our methodology employs a Planning Agent, trained with RL, to decompose a complex query into a logical sequence of sub-questions. Subsequently, an Execution Agent, trained via SFT, sys-tematically solves each sub-question using a robust multi-round retrieval and exploration process. Our experiments across multiple benchmarks demonstrate that APEX-Searcher significantly outper-forms existing methods. Ablation studies further confirmed that both the RL-based planning and SFT-based exploration stages are crucial for the model’s final performance. This work highlights the immense potential of equipping LLMs with explicit planning capabilities, paving the way for more sophisticated and effective agentic systems in complex information-seeking tasks. In the future, 1) we consider discussing whether the performance of the model in multi-round retrieval can be continuously improved through multi-round RL during the Exploration phase of multi-round retrieval; 2) expand local database retrieval to web search, and further broaden application scenarios starting from task planning.
