Abstract 

Causal discovery is often a data-driven paradigm to analyze complex real-world systems. In parallel, physics-based models such as ordinary differential equations (ODEs) provide mecha-nistic structure for many dynamical processes. Integrating these paradigms potentially allows physical knowledge to act as an inductive bias, improving identifiability, stability, and robustness of causal discovery in dynamical systems. However, such integration remains challenging: real dynamical systems often exhibit feedback, cyclic interactions, and non-stationary data trend, while many widely used causal discovery methods are formulated under acyclicity or equilibrium-based assumptions. In this work, we propose an integrative causal discovery framework for dy-namical systems that leverages partial physical knowledge as an inductive bias. Specifically, we model system evolution as a stochastic differential equation (SDE), where the drift term encodes known ODE dynamics and the diffusion term corresponds to unknown causal couplings beyond the prescribed physics. We develop a scalable sparsity-inducing MLE algorithm that exploits causal graph structure for efficient parameter estimation. Under mild conditions, we establish guarantees to recover the causal graph. Experiments on dynamical systems with diverse causal structures show that our approach improves causal graph recovery and produces more stable, physically consistent estimates than purely data-driven state-of-the-art baselines. 

# 1 Introduction 

Understanding cause-effect relationships is fundamental for explaining complex real-world phenom-ena, such as gene regulation in genetics [7, 8], anthropogenic climate change in earth science [27], and brain influence processes in neuroscience [30]. Establishing reliable causal relationships ideally relies on randomized controlled experiments (RCTs), which are often considered the gold stan-dard. However, RCTs can be prohibitively expensive, time-consuming, or even infeasible in many settings. Consequently, inferring causal structure from purely observational data or interventional data, known as causal discovery , has attracted much attention [10]. Traditional causal discov-ery methods admit intrinsic ambiguities: without additional structure, multiple causal models are statistically indistinguishable. While these assumptions have enabled substantial algorithmic progress, they are largely agnostic to the data-generating process and often misaligned with the mechanisms governing real physical systems. As a result, causal discovery algorithms may succeed statistically yet fail to recover mechanisms that are interpretable and generalizable. In contrast, physical systems are not arbitrary stochastic processes; they are constrained by conservation laws, symmetries, and dynamical principles that induce structure far stronger than generic statistical regularities. Differential equations, ordinary or partial, provide a compact rep-resentation of such constraints by encoding how variables evolve through causal interactions over time and space. This observation motivates a natural question: 

Can physical laws be formalized as an inductive bias for causal discovery, and if so, what are the theoretical consequences? 

As noted by Sch¨ olkopf et al. [31], learning reusable mechanisms is an important direction for causal discovery, and physics provides a natural source of such mechanisms. Ordinary differential equations (ODEs), in particular, are a core modeling tool for characterizing mechanistic dynamics in many scientific and engineering domains, including neuroscience [39], infectious disease [1], and manufacturing [12]. Despite the fact that both tools serve as powerful approaches for understanding real-world processes, there remains a clear need for a framework that bridges the causal discovery with available physical knowledge. 

An Intuitive Solution. Perhaps a seemingly natural approach is to incorporate physical knowledge into causal discovery by first fitting an ODE model using known mechanisms, and then 2applying a standard causal discovery algorithm to the residuals or remaining variables. While intu-itive, this strategy treats physical modeling and causal discovery as two separate stages, implicitly assuming that the known dynamics and unknown causal structure can be cleanly decoupled. In practice, this separation is often problematic: model mismatch, unmodeled feedback, and stochas-tic effects can confound residual-based causal signals, leading to unstable or spurious discoveries. Moreover, existing causal discovery methods are not designed to account for uncertainty in the physical model itself, nor to jointly reason about mechanistic and causal parameters. These limita-tions highlight the need for a unified framework in which physical constraints and causal structure are learned simultaneously. Indeed, existing approaches can struggle even in seemingly benign settings, as we demonstrate below. 

A Motivating Example. In this example, we assume that the available mechanistic knowledge is minimal such that, for a subset of variables, physics specifies only their intrinsic dynamics, which we represent as self-loops in the graph (as illustrated in the top panel of Fig.1A). This setting captures a common practical regime where reliable mechanistic information is available only at the component level, while cross-variable couplings remain uncertain. For a more general illustration using a real-world system, see §S1.2. In our formulation, the unknown causal structure is encoded in the diffusion term, and we define the target graph by the sparsity pattern of cross-variable couplings in A. The key task is to infer the unknown cross variable couplings (the green edges) between variables with unspecified dynamics and the mechanistic subsystem. Crucially, even when partially specified ODE dynamics are available, they provide mechanistic constraints on the system’s evolution, which can shrink the space of plausible interactions and reduce ambiguity relative to purely data-driven causal discovery. From this example, we can see that existing causal discovery frameworks are not designed to systematically incorporate mechanistic information, which leads to several challenges. First , many existing methods impose an acyclicity assumption , which can be overly restrictive for modeling real-world dynamical mechanisms. Second , there is a lack of a unified framework that can integrate these two sources of information. More specifically, data-driven causal discovery is often formulated via a structural causal model (SCM), such that xi(t) = fi

Since these formulations impose constraints at different levels (static functional assignments versus continuous time dynamics), it is nontrivial to combine them within a single learning objective without introducing an explicit bridge between mechanistic constraints and causal graph estimation. Our key modeling idea is to use stochastic differential equations (SDEs) to combine mechanistic knowledge and statistical knowledge. The key reason is that SDEs provide a natural extension of ODE models by accounting for process noise and unmodeled inputs. In particular, the Brownian motion component of an SDE (i.e., Wt) provides a mathematically tractable representation of ac-cumulated, small-scale random perturbations, and induces a distribution over trajectories rather than a single deterministic path. This is crucial for real data, where uncertainty must be propa-gated through time and across coupled variables . Moreover, the diffusion term separates intrinsic dynamical randomness from measurement noise, enabling principled likelihood-based inference and uncertainty quantification.

# 2 Literature Review 

Causal Discovery from Time-series Data. Our work is related to causal discovery from observational time-series data, as it studies causal structure in dynamical systems. A comprehensive survey of time-series causal discovery can be found in Gong et al. [10]. Existing approaches are typically categorized into (i) constraint-based methods relying on conditional independence tests, (ii) score-based structure optimization, and (iii) Granger-causality-based methods grounded in predictability. While our framework departs from these paradigms by incorporating mechanistic ODE knowledge as an inductive bias, it nevertheless operates on time-series observations. For completeness, we briefly review this literature in Appendix (see §S1.1). 

Interpretation of Differential Equations From Causal Perspective. One line of work that is seemingly related to our problem involves reinterpreting differential equation models from a causal perspective. For instance, Mooij et al. [22] show that, under suitable regularity conditions, the equilibrium behavior of a first-order ODE system can be mapped to a structural causal model, providing a causal interpretation of equilibrium relations, even in the presence of feedback cycles. Moreover, Hansen & Sokol [13] formalize how causal interventions act on SDEs by explicitly defining postintervention SDEs. Their framework shows that intervening on an SDE corresponds to the continuous-time limit of interventions in structural equation models, and establishes conditions under which postintervention distributions are identifiable from the observational SDE. While these works clarify how causality is defined and interpreted in differential equation models, they do not address the problem of learning causal graph structure from observational data, which is the focus of our work. 

SDE-based Causal Discovery. The closest methodological line to our work is SDE-based causal discovery, which models causal structure through stochastic dynamical systems. Bellot et al. [2] propose NGM based on the additive-noise SDE model dx (t) = f (x(t)) dt + dW (t), which uses Neural ODEs [5] for mean process f (x(t)) and extracts graphical structure from the first layer of f (x(t)). This formulation assumes data drawn from a stationary distribution induced by additive noise, which constrains its applicability to a limited class of dynamical systems. To address this limitation, Wang et al. [40] propose SCOTCH , a variational-inference framework that infers posterior distributions over candidate graphs. It adopts a general SDE formulation dx (t) = f (x(t)) dt +

g(x(t)) dW (t) and uses neural networks to parameterize both f and g, with the graph defined by the induced dependencies among state variables. However, their emphasis is on continuous-time modeling and forecasting from irregular observations. Although they discuss identifiability and consistency, their method mainly learns a latent continuous-time representation to improve prediction rather than performing classical structural identification. In addition, their diffusion term g is constrained to a nonzero diagonal form. Related efforts also include constraint-based causal discovery formulated in SDE form [20], which is currently limited to directed acyclic graphs, and optimal-control-based approaches such as graphical continuous Lyapunov models (GCLMs) 4[6, 38], which rely on additive-noise dynamics. Although these SDE-based works provide a flexible continuous time description of stochastic dynamics, existing approaches often restrict attention to additive-noise formulations, acyclicity, and frequently rely on stationarity-type conditions for analysis or inference. Moreover, ODEs and SDEs are often used primarily as data-generation tools, with limited discussion of how mechanistic ODE structure relates to the corresponding SDE representation. Importantly, in many real-world systems, ODE-level physical mechanisms constitute reusable domain knowledge. Causal discovery should be grounded in reliable prior knowledge and, when possible, refined into reusable knowledge that supports future discoveries and interventions [31]. How to systematically incorporate such partial mechanistic information as an inductive bias for causal structure learning remains an open challenge.

3.2 Characterization of Causal Influence 

Building on the previous section, we interpret the diffusion term as an uncertain information chan-nel whose impact is modulated by the current system state. In an idealized setting where the system is fully understood and all relevant mechanisms are correctly modeled, this stochastic component may be negligible. In that case, deterministic dynamics (i.e., physics-based information) can be sufficient for prediction. Consequently, Eq.(3) provides a unified formulation that recovers deter-ministic physics while incorporating causal uncertainty when unmodeled or stochastic influences are present. Under this intuition, we can define causality as in Definition 1. 5Definition 1 (Causal Coupling Beyond Known Dynamics) . With a known deterministic dynamics for Xt, an external event (or information input) exerts a causal influence beyond the known dy-namics at time t if and only if SA(Xt)̸ = 0. Equivalently, no such additional causal interaction is present if and only if the system is causally closed under the known dynamics (i.e., SA(Xt) = 0) in which case the evolution remains fully described by the known ODE. This definition characterizes causality as additional dynamical coupling not explained by the known ODE. It aligns with an information-flow interpretation of causality, but it captures higher-order (rather than pairwise) effects. More precisely, Granger causality [11] is defined operationally via incremental predictability and admits a natural interpretation as directed information flow (e.g., in terms of conditional mutual information over time-lagged histories).In our work, we leverage the availability of a partially known ODE model and define causal influence structurally: if a variable appears in the governing equations, then perturbations to that variable can propagate through the dynamics and induce changes in downstream state trajectories, thereby establishing a causal effect within the system. Under this definition, we do not need to separately impose an acyclicity assumption to improve causal interpretability. Below we provide an illustrative example based on a physical system. In addition to the conceptual discussion, §S1.4 presents a rigorous mathematical derivation of our interpretation. 

Example: Predator-prey behavior. Within a predator-prey system (e.g., foxes and rab-bits), whose population dynamics can be modeled by the Lotka-Volterra (LV) equations. In an idealized setting with no additional external drivers, the dynamics are fully determined over long time horizons given the initial conditions and fixed parameters. In practice, however, exogenous factors inevitably enter the system, for example, human hunting that reduces the predator popula-tion, habitat changes, or the introduction of a new species. These interventions can be interpreted as causal inputs that perturb the original dynamics. Consequently, the original LV model alone is no longer sufficient to capture the observed behavior, and the system must be augmented to account for these new causal factors. In our formulation, the dynamics are assumed to be known and are represented by g(t, x(t), γ) (e.g., the LV component in the predator-prey example). The additional causal coupling that is not captured by the known ODE is encoded by A.

# 5 Main Theoretical Results 

As shown in the previous section, we consider that data are generated from a discretized SDE whose distribution may be non-Gaussian due to multiplicative noise. This setting allows us to relax several standard assumptions that are commonly adopted in causal graph inference; however, it also creates fundamental technical challenges for both the formulation and the theoretical analysis. Since SDEs have a well-developed mathematical theory, we avoid imposing ad hoc assumptions directly on the observed data and instead derive the required regularity from the underlying SDE dynamics. A basic requirement is well-posedness : the SDE must admit a unique (strong) solu-tion on the observation horizon [0 , T ]; otherwise the data-generating mechanism is not identifiable and causal graph recovery is ill-defined. In the theoretical analysis, we assume the underlying ODE is linear (or locally linearizable), so that its dynamics can be written approximately as an affine mapping ˙x(t) ≈ Bx (t) + b. We therefore impose standard well-posedness conditions: (i) local Lipschitzness and linear growth of the drift and diffusion, and (ii) a square-integrable initial condition, stated precisely in §4. Beyond existence and uniqueness, for finite-sample analysis on [0 , T ] (without assuming stationarity), we also require control of the solution’s moments over time. Fortunately, Lyapunov stability provides exactly the control we need. We therefore impose the following condition.

# 6 Numerical Experiments 

In this section, we evaluate the performance of our algorithm on various datasets with diverse causal structures. Causal relationships are embedded using two graph families: DAGs and directed loop graphs. We benchmark our method against four baselines by using three evaluation metrics. 

Benchmark Methods: We evaluate our method, marked as SCD , against four time-series causal discovery baselines: DYNOTEARS [24] (score-based), PCMCI [28] (constraint-based via conditional independence tests), SCOTCH [40] (variational inference with neural SDEs and structure learning), and Neural-GC [36] (Granger causality using a regularized neural network). Evaluation Metrics: 

We evaluate graph recovery using Structural Hamming Distance (SHD), True Positive Rate (TPR), and False Discovery Rate (FDR). SHD counts edge disagreements with the ground truth (missing, extra, or misoriented edges) [37], where lower is better. TPR measures the fraction of true edges correctly identified [9], where higher is better. FDR is the fraction of predicted edges that are false positives [3], where lower is better. The data generation and experimental setup are included in 

§S1.6, and the graph generation is provided in §S1.7. 

6.1 Directed Acyclic Graphs 

We first evaluate the performance of our method on DAGs by varying the number of variables as p ∈ { 5, 10 , 15 , 20 }. The result on the stable system is reported in Fig.2, and the result for unstable system is reported in Tab.S4. The results report graph recovery for the additional coupling component, i.e., the unknown graph embedded in the diffusion term A.From Fig.2 and Tab.S4, we first observe that the SHD increases for all methods as the dimension grows from p = 5 to p = 20 under both the stable and unstable systems. This trend is expected, since the number of possible edges scales is on the order of p2, making graph recovery progressively more challenging at higher dimensions. However, SCD achieves substantially lower SHD (often by a 10 Figure 3: Results on directed loop graphs (stable system): mean/std over 10 runs (SHD, TPR, FDR). The full table is provided in Tab.S5. factor of 2-4 compared with baselines). Moreover, SCD maintains a high TPR (roughly 0 .82-0 .90) with comparatively low FDR over all cases, indicating that it recovers a large fraction of true edges without substantially inflating false discoveries. For p = 20, we observe a mild performance degradation under the unstable system across all methods (e.g., higher FDR overall). Importantly, 

SCD achieves substantially lower FDR than the baselines: its FDR is 0 .371 (stable) and 0 .404 (unstable), whereas the baselines are typically around 0 .7 in the stable case and 0 .8 in the unstable case. At p = 5, SCD also exhibits a clear advantage, with FDR 0 .067 (stable) and 0 .089 (unstable), compared to baselines that are typically around 0 .4 (stable) and 0 .6 (unstable). 

6.2 Directed Loop Graphs 

We further evaluate performance on Directed loop graphs under both stable and unstable regimes. These structures are motivated by the feedback-loop structure present in many dynamical systems as shown in Mooij et al. [22] and Fig.S1. The results have been included in Fig.3 and Tab.S6. We observe similar trends on Directed loop graphs . As p increases from 5 to 20, SHD generally increases across methods under both the stable and unstable systems. Across all dimensions and both regimes, SCD consistently achieves the lowest SHD while maintaining very high TPR (roughly 0.90 - 0 .98), indicating strong sensitivity to true edges even in the presence of feedback loops. In addition, SCD attains comparatively low FDR at lower dimensions (e.g., 0 .089 at p = 5 and 0 .143 at p = 10 in the stable case), while its FDR increases moderately at p = 20, consistent with the harder high-dimensional setting. In contrast, the baselines tend to suffer from markedly higher FDR and substantially lower TPR, especially at larger p. For example, at p = 20 in the stable loop setting (Tab.S5), SCD 

achieves SHD 15 .0 with TPR 0 .897 and FDR 0 .316, whereas the baselines typically exhibit FDR in the range 0 .73-0 .90 with TPR below 0 .13 and much larger SHD. A similar gap persists under the unstable loop setting (Tab.S6): at p = 20, SCD attains SHD 13 .5 with TPR 0 .902 and FDR 0 .274, while the baselines again show substantially higher SHD and FDR with much lower TPR. Relative to the DAG experiments, the loop-graph setting is generally more challenging for the baselines, which is consistent with an assumption mismatch. This is expected because several baselines are primarily designed for acyclic or lag-only causal structures, whereas loop graphs contain intrinsic feedback that cannot be fully captured by purely acyclic formulations. Overall, the baselines show clear failure modes for our data. DYNOTEARS typically has high FDR and low TPR, leading to large SHD. This is expected because our trajectories are generated by continuous-time integration and can violate the stationary VAR style assumptions used by 

DYNOTEARS . PCMCI achieves only moderate TPR with moderate to high FDR, and its variability increases as p grows, consistent with its reliance on stationarity. SCOTCH also exhibits high FDR and low-to-moderate TPR at larger p, likely due to a diffusion mismatch. It assumes a fully diagonal, 11 non-degenerate diffusion, whereas our data can contain near zero diffusion components. Finally, 

Neural-GC is based on Granger-style temporal precedence; feedback and contemporaneous effects in our data can violate this assumption and reduce performance. In contrast, SCD reliably recovers the graph structure and remains robust from stable to unstable dynamics. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We propose a novel and theoretically justified framework that incorporates known physics knowl-edge as an inductive bias for causal discovery, while explicitly representing uncertainty in the inferred causal graph via a principled stochastic modeling foundation based on SDEs. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first causal discovery framework for nonstationary time series that does not rely on a DAG assumption and does not require committing to a specific SDE noise structure or function class such as diagonal diffusion and additive noise. We hope this work will motivate continued exploration of frameworks that integrate mechanistic knowledge and causal reasoning for complex, nonstationary dynamical systems. 

# Impact Statement 

This work advances causal discovery by incorporating physical knowledge as an inductive bias, im-proving reliability and interpretability in dynamical systems. Ethically, grounding causal discovery in explicit physics helps reduce spurious conclusions and promotes transparent, accountable use of causal models. In the long term, more trustworthy causal discovery methods may positively impact scientific decision-making and system design across engineering and natural sciences, while emphasizing responsible deployment and interpretation of causal claims.

S1.1 Literature Review on traditional causal discovery methods 

Constraint-based methods infer causal structure via conditional independence tests. PCMCI [29] combines the PC algorithm [34] with momentary conditional independence tests to identify lagged parents, while PCMCI+ [26] further accounts for instantaneous effects. Extensions such as LPCMCI [25] handle latent confounders, and CD-NOD [41] targets nonstationary, heterogeneous time series. Without additional functional assumptions, these methods typically identify graphs only up to a Markov equivalence class. Score-based approaches instead posit an explicit SEM for the temporal process and learn the graph by optimizing a likelihood or score. Hyv¨ arinen et al. [16] leverage identifiability of additive noise models [15] to build linear autoregressive SEMs with non-Gaussian noise, and DYNOTEARS [24] adapts the continuous acyclicity constraint of Zheng et al. [42] for differentiable structure learning. Granger causality rules out backward-in-time and instantaneous effects, and is often operationalized via vector autoregressive prediction [33]. Building on this principle, Tank et al. [36] propose Neural-GC , which uses regularized neural networks for nonlin-ear Granger discovery; more recent work strengthens neural Granger methods via Jacobian-based regularization [43].

S1.6 Data Generation and experimental setup 

Data Generation: The data generation process involves two steps. (1) We construct a weighted graph G corresponding to the causal matrix A, and specify the ODE information corresponding to 

B (and b, in the linearized form). For the causal structure, the generation procedures for all three graph families are summarized in §S1.7. To ensure stability, we simulate ODEs that include all self-loops and assign the corresponding diagonal weights uniformly. Moreover, we include an additional experiment in which partial interaction terms in the ODE are assumed to be known, reflecting real-world mechanistic priors that further restrict the search space. Due to space limitations and because our primary goal is to evaluate robustness under minimal mechanistic knowledge, we report these results in the §S1.8. To probe robustness beyond the theoretical setting, we also consider unstable regimes induced by removing a subset of diagonal edges. Fig.1 visualizes one example for our setup. Moreover, stability can be verified using Theorem 3.6 of [4], which provides a practical numerical condition. (2) Given the ODE information and the specified graph structure, we generate time series data following the Euler Maruyama approximation on stochastic differential equations described in §3.1. Time series are generated on [0 , 5] with 500 time steps using the Euler Maruyama scheme (∆ t = 0 .01) for both cases. 18 Settings: For the experimental setup, we tune the penalty parameters λ and c according to our theoretical guidance. Empirically, we find that c = 0 .1 and λ = 5 perform well across the considered settings. For PCMCI and DYNOTEARS , we use a one-lag ( τ = 1) graph, since the loop structure can be represented in a lagged form under their modeling assumptions. We then aggregate the estimated lagged and contemporaneous graphs by taking the union of edges to obtain the final directed graph used for evaluation. All other settings for the remaining baselines are taken directly from the corresponding papers or their open-source implementations. After obtaining the estimated graphs, we apply thresholding to all methods. For our method, we find that a threshold in the range [0 .20 , 0.25] works well across all settings. For DYNOTEARS , the recommended thresholds in the original implementation (e.g., 0 .3 for the contemporaneous graph and 0 .1 for the lagged graph) produced degenerate outputs with no selected edges in our experiments; therefore, we use smaller thresholds, namely 0 .03 for the contemporaneous graph and 0 .01 for the lagged graph. For SCOTCH ,since its edge scores are probabilities produced by a logistic model, we threshold the output by fixing the threshold at 0 .5. All other baselines are thresholded according to their recommended settings.

S1.10 Future works 

Beyond these results, we recognize several promising directions for future work. First, although our main theory is developed under stable SDE assumptions, our empirical results indicate that the method can also perform well on unstable SDEs. This motivates a deeper theoretical study of our optimization and statistical guarantees in the unstable regime, which we believe is both important and technically interesting. Second, because SDEs form a broad and flexible modeling class, ac-commodating general drift and diffusion structures, we believe our study offers practical guidance on using SDEs as a principled tool for physics-informed causal discovery. An important and real-izable extension is to incorporate richer mechanistic knowledge by moving beyond standard ODEs to more general dynamical forms, such as delay differential equations and ODEs with time-varying parameters, as well as more general nonlinear functional specifications from a mathematical (not purely engineering) perspective. Third, stochastic partial differential equations (SPDEs) provide a natural next step for causal learning from spatiotemporal datasets, where both spatial interac-tions and temporal evolution are essential. Finally, building on the same foundational machinery, particularly Itˆ o’s lemma, a promising direction is to extend the framework to causal representation learning by introducing an observation (measurement) function on top of the latent SDE dynamics with known physics. Finally, we reiterate that our original motivation is to bring physics-informed inductive biases into causal discovery as a causal structure is reliable only insofar as the real-world phenomenon of interest can be meaningfully mapped to it. We believe that broader physics perspectives, be-yond differential equation formulations alone, should be explored, as they can further improve the reliability and applicability of causal discovery in scientific settings. While this is a compelling direction, developing a general and systematic integration of richer physical principles is inherently challenging and remains an important topic for future work. 22 S2 Supplementary Materials II: Problem Formulation and Lemma Statements
