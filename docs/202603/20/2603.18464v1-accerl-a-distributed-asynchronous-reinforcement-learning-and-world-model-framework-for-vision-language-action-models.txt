# ABSTRACT 

Reinforcement learning (RL) for large-scale Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models faces significant challenges in computational efficiency and data acquisition. We propose AcceRL, a fully asyn-chronous and decoupled RL framework designed to eliminate synchronization barriers by physically isolating training, inference, and rollouts. Crucially, AcceRL is the first to integrate a plug-and-play, trainable world model into a distributed asynchronous RL pipeline to generate virtual experiences. Ex-periments on the LIBERO [ 1 ] benchmark demonstrate that AcceRL achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. Systematically, it exhibits super-linear scaling in throughput and highly efficient hard-ware utilization. Algorithmically, the world-model-augmented variant delivers unprecedented sample efficiency and robust training stability in complex control tasks.

# 1 Introduction 

The field of embodied artificial intelligence has been profoundly transformed by the advent of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models. These models promise to create agents capable of executing complex tasks based on natural language instructions. While imitation learning has enabled significant capabilities, it suffers from poor generalization and compounding errors known as covariate shift. To overcome these barriers, the research community has turned to reinforcement learning (RL) to optimize policies for long-term success and online fine-tuning. However, applying RL to large-scale VLAs introduces severe systemic bottlenecks primarily due to the synchronization barriers in traditional frameworks. In a conventional synchronous setup, the system must frequently switch between environment interaction, policy inference, and model updates. This lockstep execution forces high-performance GPUs to idle while waiting for slow physical simulators. This creates a significant long-tail problem where the overall throughput is capped by the slowest rollout worker. Furthermore, even with optimized asynchronous execution, the training process remains constrained by the inherent low frequency of physical simulators and the extreme data hunger of large-scale VLA architectures. Relying solely on real-world or simulated interactions imposes a prohibitive temporal cost because the sampling speed is strictly limited by the simulator stepping and the model inference latency. There is an urgent need for a mechanism that can break free from the temporal constraints of physical simulation by generating high-fidelity synthetic experiences to accelerate policy refinement. To bridge this gap, we introduce AcceRL, a modular and fully asynchronous framework tailored for large-scale VLA models. AcceRL achieves a paradigm shift by physically isolating training, inference, and sampling into independent asynchronous streams. Our key contributions are as follows: • High-throughput asynchronous RL architecture: We propose a fully decoupled infrastructure that removes synchronization barriers. By eliminating frequent mode-switching and mitigating long-tail latencies, AcceRL maximizes hardware utilization and achieves super-linear scaling in system throughput. • First distributed asynchronous framework with a trainable world model: AcceRL seamlessly incorporates both world model training and plug-and-play inference pools directly into its pipeline. By enabling “learning in imagination,” the framework bypasses high-cost real-world interactions and continuously refines the world model alongside the policy. This architectural innovation improves online sample efficiency by an astonishing 

200 × (20,000%). • Empirical excellence and SOTA performance: Evaluated on the challenging LIBERO benchmark, AcceRL achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) task performance. The framework demonstrates both highly efficient systemic scalability and robust training stability in complex embodied control tasks. 

# 2 Related work 

2.1 Evolution of Distributed Reinforcement Learning Frameworks 

Historically, scaling reinforcement learning has relied on distributed frameworks to accelerate data acquisition. Even contemporary large-scale systems, such as the default veRL framework [ 3] and the synchronous baselines evaluated in RLinf [ 4] [ 5], still rely on a synchronous mechanism. In this lockstep paradigm, the central learner must wait for all distributed rollout workers to finish collecting trajectories before performing a gradient update. While this strict synchronization prevents severe trajectory staleness and maintains the bounded policy lag required by algorithms like PPO [ 6 ], the overall system throughput is dictated by the slowest worker. This phenomenon is known as the straggler effect [ 7 ], which is particularly exacerbated in large-scale generative tasks due to highly variable trajectory lengths. As a result, expensive compute resources are frequently left idle. To mitigate these synchronization overheads, frameworks evolved towards asynchronous architectures. General-purpose systems like Ray RLlib [ 8 ] introduced asynchronous task scheduling. However, their underlying architectures were fundamentally designed for lightweight models. When applied to multi-billion parameter Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models, they lack native deep integration with large-model optimization techniques such as efficient parameter offloading or model parallelism, leading to prohibitive memory bottlenecks. Addressing this coupling, IMPALA [ 9]pioneered actor-learner decoupling; yet, by retaining model inference on rollout workers, it still incurs massive memory costs when deploying such large models. Later, Laminar [ 10 ] introduced a fully decoupled architecture with trajectory-level asynchrony to solve long-tail latency. However, its design is tailored for closed-loop autoregressive generation within memory, making it structurally misaligned with the external, step-by-step causal dependencies of physical simulators required by embodied agents. Most recently, RL-VLA 3 [ 11 ] adapted these principles to Embodied AI by separating environment simulation from inference to saturate GPU utilization. While optimizing execution, it remains fundamentally bottlenecked by the slow speed of the physical simulator itself. This historical evolution highlights a 2Preprint Paper. critical unmet need for a fully decoupled architecture that can bypass the inherent latency of physical simulators to scale embodied foundation models. However, their design is primarily tailored for closed-loop autoregressive generation entirely within memory, making them structurally misaligned with the external, step-by-step causal dependencies required by embodied agents interacting with physical simulators. To bridge this gap, domain-specific frameworks have emerged. For instance, RLinf-VLA [ 5 ] introduces a hybrid fine-grained pipeline to overlap rendering and training for VLA models. Yet, it remains fundamentally bottlenecked by the inherently slow stepping speed of the external physical simulator itself. Similarly, RL-VLA 3 [11 ] attempts to saturate GPU utilization by strictly decoupling environment simulation from model inference via asynchronous queues. While optimizing raw execution speed, this asynchronous architecture exposes two critical vulnerabilities: it inevitably introduces severe trajectory staleness and massive cross-node communication overhead for high-dimensional multimodal data, thereby significantly degrading sample efficiency. In contrast, AcceRL extends this architectural decoupling to a fundamentally new paradigm by integrating a plug-and-play World Model. This allows the system to bypass the high-latency physical simulators through zero-offload, distributed imagination rollouts. By permanently keeping both the policy and world models on dedicated GPU inference pools, AcceRL eliminates the severe VRAM swapping bottlenecks of collocated execution while resolving the external I/O dependency. This evolution—from strict synchronous locking, to compromised partial decoupling, and finally to AcceRL’s unified separation of training, inference, and scalable world-model simulation—highlights a robust pathway for scaling embodied foundation models without sacrificing algorithmic fidelity. 

2.2 Training and Optimization of Vision-Language-Action Models 

The remarkable reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently been augmented with visual encoders, giving rise to Vision-Language Models (VLMs). Integrating these multimodal foundation models into robotics has created a new paradigm where physical control is treated as a sequence modeling task. Pioneering frameworks like RT-1 [ 12 ] and RT-2 [ 13 ] demonstrate that Transformer architectures can control robots by discretizing actions into text-like tokens. These models are primarily trained using Behavior Cloning (BC) on large offline datasets. However, BC suffers from a fundamental limitation known as covariate shift [ 14 ]; small errors during execution lead the robot to unfamiliar states, causing compounding failures that static training data cannot correct. To address these errors, Reinforcement Learning (RL) is often required for online fine-tuning. However, applying RL to VLA models introduces severe efficiency bottlenecks. For instance, recent works like SimpleVLA-RL [ 15 ] have attempted to adapt LLM-based RL frameworks to VLA models, demonstrating performance gains using outcome-based rewards. However, SimpleVLA-RL stands as a typical representative of the synchronous training scheme. Its fundamental reliance on a strict collect-then-update mechanism dictates that the central learner must consistently halt and wait for all distributed rollout workers to finish their environment interactions before parameter updating. Due to the inherent low frequency of physical simulators coupled with the heavy inference latency of multi-billion parameter VLAs, this synchronous barrier causes severe GPU idling and straggler effects. Consequently, the training process becomes prohibitively slow and computationally inefficient. To lower the hardware barrier, recent open-source efforts like OpenVLA [ 16 ] and Octo [ 17 ] often incorporate parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) and quantization. While these methods lower the hardware barrier for training, they do not solve the inference latency problem. RL fine-tuning necessitates generating millions of rollout steps. Furthermore, as modern VLAs increasingly adopt multi-step diffusion or flow-based action heads, applying off-policy RL introduces severe algorithmic instabilities, such as exploding gradients. While recent breakthroughs like SAC Flow [ 18 ] elegantly mitigate these issues via velocity-reparameterized sequential modeling, the underlying system-level bottleneck remains unaddressed: the sheer latency of generating rollouts with multi-step generative policies renders synchronous training pipelines severely underutilized. For a massive 7B-parameter model, running this many forward passes on a physical simulator is computationally expensive and prohibitively slow. This highlights the dual need for better sample efficiency and a decoupled system architecture. World Models [ 19 , 20 ]offer a solution by learning to predict the environment’s dynamics. Instead of interacting with a slow physical simulator, the agent can learn inside a fast, latent dream space. Although generative environments like Genie [ 21 ] show great potential, integrating them into a distributed VLA training system remains a challenge. AcceRL bridges this gap by using a parallelized World Model to replace physical simulation, enabling efficient RL fine-tuning for large-scale embodied models. 

2.3 World Models and Model-Based Reinforcement Learning 

Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL) fundamentally accelerates convergence by learning a predictive model of the environment’s dynamics, effectively substituting physical interactions with computational predictions. Early 3Preprint Paper. approaches like the Dreamer series [ 20 ] projected observations into a compact latent space, using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to predict future states st+1 and rewards rt+1 . By optimizing the policy within this differentiable dream, agents can experience millions of transition steps without a single real-world interaction, dramatically improving sample efficiency. With the advent of generative AI, environmental prediction has evolved from simple latent dynamics to high-fidelity video generation powered by Diffusion Models [ 22 ]. Frameworks such as Genie [ 21 ] and Diamonds [ 23 ] leverage the iterative denoising process to model complex, multi-modal distributions of future frames. This capability is particularly critical for VLA models, as it allows the agent to visualize the consequences of fine-grained manipulation tasks that are difficult to capture with low-dimensional latent states. These diffusion-based world models act as an infinite, risk-free simulator, providing a dense stream of virtual samples that cover edge cases rarely encountered in the physical world. However, integrating these powerful world models into the VLA training loop introduces major system-level bottlenecks due to VRAM constraints. Recent frameworks like WoVR [ 24 ] attempt to enable world-model-based RL for VLAs, but they rely on a collocated GPU allocation strategy. To avoid out-of-memory errors, these systems force synchronous phase transitions. This process is heavily bottlenecked by repeatedly moving model parameters between GPU and CPU memory (offloading and onloading). During these memory transfers, the GPU compute cores sit completely idle, causing severe hardware underutilization and low training throughput. In contrast, AcceRL solves this problem by completely abandoning collocated execution. Instead, it uses a fully decoupled, physically isolated architecture that permanently keeps the policy and world models on dedicated GPUs. This “zero-offload” design reduces network communication to passing lightweight trajectory data. By doing so, AcceRL ensures that the GPU compute cores are continuously saturated, unlocking near-linear scalability for distributed rollout generation and remarkable super-linear training throughput for massive foundation models.

3.1 Macro-Asynchrony: Decoupling Training and Rollout 

To fundamentally resolve the cluster-level long-tail and eliminate global synchronization overhead, AcceRL introduces its first level of asynchrony: decoupling the Rollout Workers from the Trainer Workers. Unlike synchronous architectures that enforce a strict lockstep of “collect-then-update” across all nodes, AcceRL employs a non-blocking circular pipeline. Instead of waiting for all workers to finish their data collection quotas, Rollout Workers interact with environments independently and continuously stream completed trajectories to a distributed replay buffer B. In parallel, Trainer Workers sample batches from B and update parameters continuously without ever blocking the data generation process. To further optimize large-scale training, the models within the Trainer Workers utilize ZeRO [ 2 ] to partition optimizer states and gradients across available GPUs, significantly improving memory efficiency. Upon the completion of each training batch, the updated parameters are synchronized to the corresponding Inference Actors via a high-performance NCCL broadcast group, ensuring that the rollout policy remains current without interrupting the training flow. By removing global synchronization barriers, GPUs minimize idle time waiting for data, allowing training to proceed at a steadier and more efficient pace. 

3.2 Micro-Asynchrony: Decoupling Interaction and Inference 

While macro-asynchrony removes global barriers, the second level of asynchrony addresses the inefficiency within the rollout process by eliminating the micro-level idle bubbles caused by step-level and episode-level long-tails. In traditional setups, a worker sequentially executes physical simulation steps and model inference. Because the computation time for distinct environment steps and the total horizon of different episodes vary significantly, this tightly coupled execution leads to frequent context switching, severe straggler effects, and poor GPU batching. To overcome this inefficiency, AcceRL adopts an Inference-as-a-Service paradigm to completely decouple physical interaction from policy inference. Each Rollout Worker manages an environment instance. When an observation ot is generated, instead of computing the action locally, the worker sends an asynchronous request to a centralized Inference Pool and immediately suspends or handles other tasks. 3.5 Asynchronous Parallel Data Prefetching 

To address the I/O bottlenecks inherent in experience replay sampling and trajectory processing, we implement a data prefetching architecture based on asynchronous parallel logic. By maintaining a background asynchronous loop within the trainer that operates independently of the primary training task, AcceRL achieves a effective decoupling of data preparation from gradient updates. Functioning as a producer, this background process continuously monitors the distributed replay buffer and triggers cross-node trajectory sampling once the required threshold is met. This concurrency ensures that while the GPU executes gradient computations for the current batch, computational resources are simultaneously preparing the data for the subsequent iteration. Furthermore, AcceRL simplifies the training critical path by delegating real-time advantage recomputation, observation tensorization, and batching operations to the background prefetcher. By stripping these complex inference and transformation tasks from the main loop, the system establishes a robust producer-consumer model where the trainer extracts ready-to-use super-batches directly from a local cache. This mechanism effectively achieves near-zero-latency data loading, maximizing hardware utilization and accelerating convergence in complex embodied tasks. 

# 4 AcceRL with World Model 

AcceRL can further integrate a world model, which consists of an observation model Mobs and a reward model Mreward ,as shown in Figure 3b. Specifically, Mobs is responsible for predicting the next-frame observation (which corresponds to high-fidelity pixel-level images in our experiments) to simulate environment dynamics. To support this architecture, we deploy independent TrainerWorkers and InferenceWorkers for both Mobs and Mreward . Furthermore, the replay buffer is partitioned into two distinct components: a world model buffer Bwm to store real state transitions to train the world model, and an imaginary replay buffer Bimg to store synthetic state trajectories generated by the world model for policy optimization. The RolloutWorkers operate via an alternating sampling strategy where the generation of numerous imagined trajectory episodes within the world model is interleaved with the collection of a single trajectory from the real-world environment. The rollout in real environment serves as a collection process for both world model training data and initial state of imaginary. Initially, the worker dynamically selects an environment based on the failure rates. It then dispatches current observation ot to the Mpolicy in the InferenceWorker Pool to get action at, which is executed to collect real sequences of observation ot+1 and reward rt. Upon task completion, the worker stores the trajectory τ in episode buffer to serve as starting observation for imagination rollouts. # 5 Implementation Refinements for VLA RL 

5.1 Vocabulary Slimming 

To efficiently adapt the OpenVLA-OFT [ 26 ] (based on the Llama-2-7B language backbone) for RL, we prune the language model head lm _head to output only the action vocabulary (e.g., 256 bins), decoupling the policy from the full language lexicon. This reduces VRAM and compute costs while preventing probability mass from leaking to non-action tokens. AcceRL employs an in-place replacement strategy to crop the original, massive output layer lm _head into a compact linear layer containing only action-related tokens. Specifically, the system first locates the start index and end index of the action tokens in the original vocabulary, then clones the corresponding weights from the original lm _head.weight 

via slicing. Finally, a new linear layer with an output dimension of Nactions is created, and the extracted weights are migrated to this layer, directly overwriting the original language model head. By slimming the vocabluary, the model only needs to output action commands and does not need to process language text, provides the following advantages: • VRAM Efficiency: Reducing the weight matrix from [V, d model ] (where V ≈ 32 k) to [Nactions , d model ]

significantly reduces the existing occupancy of gradients and optimizer states. • Increased throughput: Eliminating most of the computational overhead of the last layer significantly speeds up the iteration frequency of each training step. • Noise Reduction: Physically isolating non-action tokens prevents the model from assigning probability to irrelevant vocabulary, focusing the policy distribution and speeding up convergence.

5.3 Token-level Policy Optimization 

Defining the granularity of the optimization objective is critical for training stability, leading us to distinguish between three primary approaches: chunk-level, action-level, and our adopted token-level optimization. Standard chunk-level methods treat the entire sequence of K tokens as a single unified action. The joint probability is calculated as P (A|s) = QKk=1 π(ak|s, a <k ), which frequently leads to severe numerical instability. Because the joint probability is a product of multiple values between 0 and 1, the resulting magnitude can become infinitesimally small, causing the PPO importance sampling ratio rt(θ) = πθ (A|s)

to fluctuate wildly. Such fluctuations often trigger the PPO clipping mechanism, leading to a total loss of gradient signals and stalled training. Alternatively, an action-level approach might compute the loss based on the mean or aggregated probability of the tokens within a single action dimension. However, this still risks blurring the fine-grained causal dependencies between individual tokens in the VLA’s auto-regressive generation process. In contrast, we implement a token-level optimization strategy. By treating each token as an independent decision point, the importance sampling ratio is redefined for each individual token:

πθold (ak|s, a <k ) (17) By calculating the clip loss at the token level, we prevent outlier tokens from causing the entire action chunk’s gradient to be clipped, thereby retaining a much higher density of training signals. This approach ensures numerical robustness by avoiding the vanishing product problem and provides precise credit assignment to specific components of the action sequence. Furthermore, the dependency a<k is inherently maintained by the VLA’s causal self-attention mechanism, ensuring that each token-level ratio accurately reflects the conditional probability shift while enabling stable policy evolution on large scale VLA backbones.

# 7 Conclusion 

This paper presents AcceRL, an asynchronous reinforcement learning framework optimized for large scale VLA. The core strength of the framework lies in its trainable and plug and play world model module. This integration facilitates high fidelity imagination rollouts that allow the system to transcend the frequency constraints of physical simulators while significantly enhancing sample efficiency. Furthermore AcceRL achieves superior performance by leveraging a fully decoupled architecture for rollout and inference and training. This design effectively eliminates systemic bottlenecks such as synchronization overhead and memory constraints. By ensuring high hardware utilization, it unlocks near-linear scalability for distributed rollouts and super-linear throughput for the trainer as the cluster expands. Although the framework demonstrates high efficiency it does not yet support the post training of large scale language models. Future research will focus on extending the architecture to accommodate full scale model alignment.
