## Abstract 

Knowledge editing (KE) aims to precisely rectify specific knowledge in Large Language Models (LLMs) without disrupting general capabilities. State-of-the-art methods suffer from an open-loop control mismatch. We identify a critical 

‚ÄúSemantic-Execution Disconnect‚ÄĚ : the semantic target is derived independently without feedback from the downstream‚Äôs feasible region. This misalignment of-ten causes valid semantic targets to fall within the prohibited space, resulting in gradient truncation and editing failure. To bridge this gap, we propose MetaKE 

(Meta -learning Aligned Knowledge Editing), a new framework that reframes KE as a bi-level optimization problem. Departing from static calculation, MetaKE treats the edit target as a learnable meta-parameter: the upper-level optimizer seeks a feasible target to maximize post-edit performance, while the lower-level solver executes the editing. To address the challenge of differentiating through complex solvers, we derive a Structural Gradient Proxy , which explicitly backpropagates editability constraints to the target learning phase. Theoretical analysis demon-strates that MetaKE automatically aligns the edit direction with the model‚Äôs feasible manifold. Extensive experiments confirm that MetaKE significantly outperforms strong baselines, offering a new perspective on knowledge editing. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have become a core substrate for modern NLP systems [ 1‚Äď 3], yet their parametric memory inevitably contains factual errors and outdated knowledge [ 4‚Äď 6]. Knowl-edge editing (KE) aims to rectify a set of targeted behaviors while preserving the model‚Äôs general capabilities [ 7 , 8]. Despite substantial progress, reliable editing under strong constraints remains elusive: edits that succeed semantically may still fail physically, and improvements in edit success often come at the expense of locality or stability [9, 10]. Dominant KE methods such as ROME [ 11 ], MEMIT [ 12 ], and AlphaEdit [ 13 ] follow a decoupled 

Compute-then-Solve routine: a Semantic Planning stage first produces a semantic target v‚ąó, and a 

Editing Execution stage then solves a constrained least-squares update to realize this target in weights. We identify a fundamental limitation of this open-loop design, termed the Semantic-Execution Disconnect : v‚ąó is optimized without awareness of the preservation constraints enforced by the downstream [ 14 ]. Modern solvers preserve memories by suppressing sensitive update directions, e.g., via inverse-covariance regularization (MEMIT) or orthogonal projections (AlphaEdit). When the semantically ideal v‚ąó demands movement along these protected directions, the solver must dampen

This reveals an implicit and often violated assumption: the semantically optimal direction lies within the solver‚Äôs feasible update set under preservation constraints, which can break down in dense, deep representations. While meta-editors (e.g., MEND [ 15 ], ENN [ 16 ]) and adaptive gradient guidance methods (e.g., AGRADE [ 17 ]) alleviate parts of this tension, reliably achieving strong edits under preservation constraints without sacrificing locality and stability remains challenging. To bridge this gap, we propose MetaKE ( Meta -learning Aligned Knowledge Editing), which recasts knowledge editing as a bi-level optimization problem. MetaKE treats the edit target v‚ąó as a learnable meta-parameter: the upper level optimizes v‚ąó for edit success, while the lower level enforces the solver‚Äôs preservation constraints, providing gradient feedback that aligns v‚ąó with the feasible update manifold. MetaKE treats the semantic target as a learnable 

meta-parameter , which enables the optimization process to proactively sense downstream physical constraints, resolving edit failures caused by conflict-induced signal attenuation. ‚ÄĘ We introduce the Structural Gradient Proxy. By distilling the closed-form solution of the constraints into a differentiable module, we convert physical barriers into a ‚ÄúGeometric Gating Gradient‚ÄĚ. This mechanism efficiently filters out gradient components pointing towards the constraint space, automatically rectifying the optimization direction into the parameter‚Äôs feasible Range Space without the need for expensive full-solver unrolling. ‚ÄĘ We provide theoretical analysis demonstrating that the gradient flow guided by our structural proxy achieves asymptotic alignment with the Feasible Manifold of the parameter space, effectively mitigating the Information Truncation phenomenon observed in strong-constraint settings. Em-pirically, MetaKE significantly boosts editing success rates and generalization while maintaining preservation of unrelated knowledge. 

## 2 Related Work 

Locate-then-Edit and Mass-Editing Constraints The ‚ÄúLocate-then-Edit‚ÄĚ paradigm remains the standard for KE [ 11 , 18 ]. ROME localizes factual recall to rank-one updates [ 11 ], while MEMIT scales least-squares editing across layers with inverse-covariance regularization to preserve general knowledge [ 12 ]. Beyond soft regularization, recent work increasingly imposes explicit geometric constraints: PMET and EMMET formulate equality-constrained solvers to reduce collateral damage and balance stability‚Äďplasticity [ 19 , 20 ], and AlphaEdit enforces orthogonality via null-space pro-jection [ 13 ]. Despite stronger constraints in Physical Execution , these methods remain open-loop: they accept an upstream semantic target without accounting for how constraints truncate semantically ideal updates, yielding Spectral Suppression .

Meta-Learning and Objective-Driven Optimization Another line learns update mappings or improves objectives. Hypernetwork approaches (e.g., MEND; KE) predict weight updates from gradients [ 7, 15 ], and MALMEN approximates least-squares solutions under varying batch sizes [ 21 ]. More recently, target-centric methods optimize what to write: AdaEdit adds future sequence probability constraints [ 22 ], AnyEdit extends this to long-form autoregressive editing via iterative key-token updates [ 23 ], and REP dynamically optimizes robust editing keys to decouple edits from native representations [ 24 ]. In contrast, MetaKE is a meta-optimizer that closes the loop with the 

analytical structure of constraint-based solvers. While AdaEdit/AnyEdit mainly optimize targets for linguistic coherence (Semantic Disconnect), MetaKE optimizes targets for physical realizability 

under hard constraints (Physical Disconnect), aligning planned targets with the feasible geometry via gradient feedback through the solver. 23 Preliminaries

Physical Realizability and Anisotropic Feasibility. Standard editing often fails when the required update lies in a protected subspace, leading to spectral suppression (attenuation factor ő≤ ‚Č™ 1). MetaKE circumvents this by treating v‚ąó as a learnable parameter. The meta-gradient ‚ąáv‚ąó Lmeta 

pushes v‚ąó away from suppression-triggering directions toward regions where ő≤ ‚Čą 1 (Fig. 1A). Unlike static regularizers őĽ that create an ‚Äúimpossible gap‚ÄĚ between hard and easy edits, MetaKE embeds feasibility into the gradient flow via the Structural Gate M T . Each edit is adapted to the anisotropic feasible manifold : hard edits (high spectral density) are tightly bounded by the safety limit to preserve locality, while easy edits can safely extend to achieve the required progress (Fig. 1B).
