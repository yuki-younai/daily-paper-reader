# Continual Learning in Large Language Models: Methods, Challenges, and Opportunities 

Hongyang Chen *, Zhongwu Sun, Hongfei Ye, Kunchi Li, Xuemin Lin 

Abstract —Continual learning (CL) has emerged as a pivotal paradigm to enable large language models (LLMs) to dynam-ically adapt to evolving knowledge and sequential tasks while mitigating catastrophic forgetting—a critical limitation of the static pre-training paradigm inherent to modern LLMs. This survey presents a comprehensive overview of CL methodolo-gies tailored for LLMs, structured around three core training stages: continual pre-training, continual fine-tuning, and con-tinual alignment.Beyond the canonical taxonomy of rehearsal-, regularization-, and architecture-based methods, we further subdivide each category by its distinct forgetting mitigation mechanisms and conduct a rigorous comparative analysis of the adaptability and critical improvements of traditional CL methods for LLMs. In doing so, we explicitly highlight core distinctions between LLM CL and traditional machine learning, particu-larly with respect to scale, parameter efficiency, and emergent capabilities. Our analysis covers essential evaluation metrics, including forgetting rates and knowledge transfer efficiency, along with emerging benchmarks for assessing CL performance. This survey reveals that while current methods demonstrate promising results in specific domains, fundamental challenges persist in achieving seamless knowledge integration across diverse tasks and temporal scales. This systematic review contributes to the growing body of knowledge on LLM adaptation, providing researchers and practitioners with a structured framework for understanding current achievements and future opportunities in lifelong learning for language models. 

Index Terms —Large language models, Continual learning, Neural language processing, Catastrophic forgetting 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Since the introduction of the Transformer architecture [1], a plethora of Transformer-based pre-trained language mod-els have been proposed and widely adopted, exemplified by seminal works such as BERT [2], GPT [3], and LLaMA [4]. By undergoing massive-scale pre-training on diverse corpora, these large language models (LLMs) exhibit emergent capabil-ities, which serves as direct empirical evidence for the scaling law—the principle that model performance and generalization improve systematically with increases in parameter count and training data volume. As a result, LLMs have achieved state-of-the-art performance across a wide range of natural language processing (NLP) tasks, demonstrating strong generalization, in-context learning, and instruction-following abilities, thereby

establishing a transformative paradigm for modern NLP re-search [5]. However, the success of LLMs is inherently contingent upon a static pre-training paradigm, which stands in sharp contrast to the dynamic, ever-evolving nature of real-world scenarios. First, the real world is characterized by perpetual flux: new knowledge, concepts, events, and linguistic usages emerge in-cessantly, rendering it infeasible to curate a single, exhaustive dataset that encapsulates the full spectrum of potential infor-mation. Second, stringent data privacy regulations and ethical constraints preclude the collection of sensitive or proprietary data, further undermining the completeness and comprehen-siveness of pre-training corpora. Third, re-pre-training LLMs on updated data is prohibitively expensive— the massive scale of both models and datasets incurs substantial computational and financial burdens, rendering full retraining impractical for frequent knowledge updates. In contrast, the human brain exhibits a remarkable capacity for incremental learning: it acquires new knowledge without catastrophic forgetting of prior skills, while adapting existing knowledge to align with novel situations. Inspired by this biological intelligence, there is an urgent imperative for large language models (LLMs) to emulate such a capability—specifically, to incrementally learn from new data or tasks, retain pre-acquired knowledge, and dy-namically adapt to evolving real-world scenarios. This critical misalignment between LLMs’ static pre-training paradigm and the inherent dynamicity of real-world knowledge has firmly established continual learning for LLMs as a pivotal, high-priority research direction. As a well-established paradigm across machine learning (ML), computer vision (CV), and natural language processing (NLP) [6], continual learning enables neural networks to sequentially learn a sequence of domain corpora or end-tasks, and for LLMs, it has emerged as a promising solution to incorporate new knowledge at minimal cost while preserv-ing existing expertise. Empirical evidence [7] corroborates that applying continual learning methodologies to pre-trained LLMs not only updates their internal knowledge repositories but also substantially boosts task-specific performance. Com-pared to training from scratch, continual learning leverages pre-acquired knowledge to drastically reduce computational overhead and improve training efficiency, while sustaining state-of-the-art performance—this unique synergy of efficiency and adaptability lays a robust foundation for LLM deployment in real-world scenarios, directly addressing the core challenge of static pre-training amid dynamic knowledge evolution. A. Large language models 

Following the success of foundational models like GPT[3] and LLaMA[4], even more powerful large language models (LLMs) have emerged, such as Claude[11], Qwen[12], and DeepSeek[13], further accelerating the advancement of nat-ural language processing. The rapid development of LLMs has drawn significant attention, largely due to their massive parameter counts and impressive emergent capabilities. The training of LLMs primarily involves three stages: pre-training, fine-tuning, and alignment. In the pre-training stage, large language models (LLMs) are trained in a self-supervised manner on extensive corpora, which typically encompass diverse text sources such as books, websites, academic papers, and other publicly accessible con-tent [14]. The primary objective of this stage is to equip the model with a foundational understanding of linguistic structures and semantics by learning to predict subsequent tokens in a sequence. Specifically, the optimization goal during pre-training often revolves around minimizing a loss function associated with next-token prediction, such as the cross-entropy loss. In the fine-tuning stage, large language models (LLMs) are further refined using high-quality instruction datasets [16]. This stage not only enhances the model’s performance on spe-cific tasks but also improves its ability to follow instructions, making it more reliable and versatile for practical applications. Among the various fine-tuning approaches, Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is the most widely adopted method. Many open-source LLMs undergo SFT on small, domain-specific datasets to adapt them for downstream applications in specialized fields. The optimization goal during supervised fine-tuning typically focuses on aligning the model’s outputs with human-provided instructions or desired responses. B. Traditional continual learning 

Continual learning, also known as lifelong learning and incremental learning, is an advanced machine learning algo-rithm that learns continuously, accumulates knowledge from the past, and adapts it to facilitate future learning [20]. It aims to train sequentially on a series of tasks, ensuring that performance on previous tasks does not degrade while training on new tasks. The main objectives of continual learning are to avoid catastrophic forgetting and facilitate knowledge transfer. 4

Catastrophic forgetting [21] refers to the sharp decline in per-formance on previous tasks when the model is trained on new tasks. Knowledge transfer includes both forward and backward transfer. Forward transfer means using the knowledge learned from previous tasks to aid in learning new tasks, while backward transfer refers to the improvement of performance on previous tasks through the knowledge obtained from new tasks. In traditional machine learning, three primary approaches have been proposed to address the challenges of CL: rehearsal-based, regularization-based, and architecture-based methods: 

Rehearsal-based methods. These methods store the samples from the previous tasks or generate the pseudo-samples by a generator to alleviate the catastrophic forgetting. However, these methods can be especially intractable for the privacy problems and need more storage burdens. 

Regularization-based methods. The main idea of regularization-based methods is to add penalty terms or regularization to the loss function to restrict the updates to the model’s important parameters to prevent catastrophic forgetting. HAT [22] is a continual learning method in computer vision that leverages a hard attention mechanism and task-specific identifiers to restrict gradient flow between neurons to address catastrophic forgetting. Similarly, SPG [23] proposed a soft-mask method by computing the importance score of each unit to constrain the gradient flow only in the backward pass to encourage knowledge sharing. 

Architecture-based methods. These methods facilitate con-tinual learning by expanding the model architecture. Specifi-cally, they typically introduce additional parameters into the model. By freezing the original model weights and training only on the new parameters, they help prevent catastrophic forgetting. CTR[24] is built upon the pre-trained BERT model and incorporates CL-plugins, where each CL-plugin is acapsule[25] network equipped with a routing algorithm to identify and transfer knowledge across tasks. It has demon-strated outstanding performance in traditional NLP tasks. For each new task, Supsup [26] identifies a supermask within the network to generate a subnetwork specific to the task.. 

C. Continual learning in Large Language Models 

Although the LLMs have shown powerful emergent capa-bilities, they still need to continually learn new knowledge and tasks to adapt to the ever-changing real world. Continual learning on LLMs enables the acquisition of new knowledge while preserving existing knowledge. The core challenges of continual learning are to learn new knowledge and overcome catastrophic forgetting French [21] in the previous tasks. We categorize continual learning for large language models into three categories: continual pre-training, continual fine-tuning, and continual alignment, corresponding to the three training stages of large models. Table I provides an overall view of these methods. 

1) Continual pre-training: In the continual pre-training stage, models are typically trained on large-scale unlabeled corpus. Continual pre-training involves two parts: horizontally pre-training the model using new data with a similar distribu-tion to the pre-training data, to learn new general knowledge. Vertically pre-training the model in specific domains to adapt it to a sequence of domains, such as the legal domain, the medical domain, the financial domain and so on, which is also known as domain-adaptive pre-training. 

2) Continual fine-tuning: Continual fine-tuning, also re-ferred to as continual instruction fine-tuning, involves incre-mentally adapting a model to a sequence of downstream tasks. These tasks typically span various application scenarios and requirements, such as text classification, text generation, and text summarization. During this phase, the model is fine-tuned sequentially on downstream instruction datasets, where previously used datasets are no longer accessible. The goal is to ensure the model assimilates knowledge from all tasks and maintains strong performance across them. A critical challenge in continual fine-tuning is to enable the model to learn new task-specific knowledge while preserving its performance on earlier tasks, thereby avoiding catastrophic forgetting. This ensures the model retains its prior capabilities while acquiring new ones. 

3) Continual alignment: During the continual alignment phase, the primary training objective is to continuously op-timize the values underlying the model’s generated content, ensuring that its outputs remain aligned with dynamically evolving societal values, ethical norms, and user preferences. By regularly updating and fine-tuning the model, continual alignment ensures consistency with contemporary social de-mands and value systems, enhancing the model’s applicability and credibility in real-world scenarios. Current alignment methods are primarily categorized into reinforcement learning approaches, such as RLHF [18], and non-reinforcement learn-ing approaches, such as DPO [19]. However, these methods require retraining when applied to continual learning scenarios, which is often impractical due to computational resource constraints and data privacy challenges. 5

III. C ONTINUAL PRE -TRAINING 

Updating the knowledge and capabilities of a pre-trained foundational model is important. However, retraining the LLMs from scratch requires enormous data source and com-puting resource, means it impractical. Continual pre-training can incrementally update the internal knowledge of a pre-trained model and preserving its original language capabilities. By incorporating the latest data or domain-specific datasets, the model’s internal knowledge is updated, thereby enhanc-ing its capabilities and improving its end-task performance. Effectively minimizing the need for completely retraining the model while enhancing its ability to generalize to new tasks and domains. Continual pre-training, also known as domain-adaptive pre-training, utilizes a sequence of unlabeled domain corpora to progressively adapt a pre-trained language model to various domains. Gururangan et al. [7] demonstrates that continual pre-training enables models to maintain their fundamental understanding of language and facilitates the incremental acquisition of new domain knowledge, which is crucial for effective communication and comprehension. It can make models become more adaptable to evolving information and can effectively address specialized tasks that require updated or domain-specific insights. This dual capability enhances their performance in dynamic environments where information is constantly changing. The primary challenge of continual pre-training is to prevent the decline of the model’s capabilities in previously learned general domains[48]. When a model is trained on a new corpus, it often tends to forget a portion of the knowledge acquired from the old dataset, a phenomenon known as catastrophic forgetting. This issue is particularly concerning because it could undermine the model’s effectiveness in tasks where a broad understanding of language is essential. To address this, numerous strategies have been proposed to enable the model to retain previously learned information while simultaneously integrating new knowledge, ensuring that the model remains proficient in both established and emerging areas of expertise. In the field of continual learning for large language models, several comprehensive review studies have been conducted. Most previous work has categorized continual pre-training papers based on different domains, such as legal, medical, and financial. However, we introduce an innovative approach by classifying these papers according to the specific techniques employed in continual pre-training. We propose three distinct categories: rehearsal-based methods, data augmentation meth-ods, and process optimization methods. This classification not only highlights the diverse methodologies within the continual pre-training landscape but also provides a clearer framework for understanding how different approaches can enhance the learning capabilities of language models over time. Table I provides an overall summary of continual pre-training meth-ods. 

A. Rehearsal-based methods 

The rehearsal-based methods involve incorporating a mix-ture of previously used training data during the continual pre-training process. Specifically, during continual pre-training, a subset of the data from earlier training phases is mixed back into the model’s training dataset. These data is sampled from the model’s prior pre-training data and serves as a form of memory. Although the rehearsal-based methods are simple, they are comprehensively used in continual pre-training and are highly effective in mitigating catastrophic forgetting, enabling models to retain previously learned information while continually adapting to new domains. 6

Fujii et al. [27], Cui et al. [28] transfer the base model to other language domains with higher performance than the model trained from scratch by utilizing a mixture of new language data and pre-trained data. Fujii et al. [27] developed a Japanese language model by continual pre-training LLaMA2 on a mixture of Japanese and English datasets, and demon-strated the model performance improves in line with increases in training data. Cui et al. [28] continual pre-training the LLaMA2 on Chinese data,leading to significant improvements in Chinese text understanding and generation. Building on prior scaling laws [49, 50], recent studies have extended these principles to continual pre-training. [30] focused on cross-lingual CPT, conducting experiments on 40 model sizes to analyze scaling behaviors across different languages. Meanwhile, [29] investigated domain-specific CPT in specialized fields such as code, math, law, and others, proposing a parameterized domain-continual pre-training (D-CPT) law. [31] proposed critical mixture ratios for continual pre-training and considered the trade-off between general loss and domain loss. These works examined the impact of data mixture ratios on downstream task performance across various model sizes and training token scales, offering insights into optimizing data mixing ratios to improve training efficiency and performance under constraints of limited computational and data resources. Collectively, these studies demonstrate that CPT converges faster and requires fewer computational resources compared to training models from scratch. Similarity, Xi et al. [32] investigates the optimization of the additional language mixture ratio and learning rate during the continual pre-training of the large-scale language model LLaMA3-70B in the Chinese domain. The results show model capability improves not only in the additional language, but also in the original language and even in other specific domains. The integration of these two data sources enables the model to leverage foundational knowledge acquired during pre-training while adapting to the specific characteristics of the new domain. This approach facilitates a balanced trade-off between retaining prior knowledge and assimilating new information, which is essential for maintaining robust perfor-mance in dynamic environments. 

B. Data augmentation methods 

In the continual pre-training of large language models, data quality plays a critical role in determining the model’s final performance. High-quality data enables models to more effectively acquire knowledge and enhance their performance. A variety of methods have been developed to improve the quality of data used during continual pre-training, thereby boosting the model’s ultimate performance. Cheng et al. [33] proposed a rule-based data augmentation method to transform raw corpora into reading comprehension texts. Each raw text is enriched with a series of question-answer pair related to it content. This rule-based method mines intrinsic tasks through a few regex-based patterns and reinforce the model’s ability to respond to prompts effectively while also integrating domain knowledge. Similarly, Cheng et al. [34] trains an instruction synthesizer to convert raw text into question-answer pair instruction data. The synthesized instructions are subsequently combined with the raw text data for continual pre-training. The Mix-CPT [35] decouples knowledge learning and format alignment by combining the raw domain corpus, general instructions, and alignment data to enhance knowledge learning ability. Specifically, it incor-porates a self-distillation constraint to alleviate catastrophic forgetting. Xie et al. [36] introduces FinPythia-6.9B, which is obtained via continual pre-training in the financial domain.It proposes efficient data selection methods ETS-DACP and ETA-DACP for continual pre-training, which outperform vanilla continual pre-training’s performance with just 10% of data cost, without any degradation on open-domain standard tasks. Guo et al. [37] identifying a ”stability gap” phenomenon where initial performance drops before recovering in continual pre-training and proposing three data augmentation strategies to mitigate it. It introduces Llama3-Physician, a model trained on the medical domain, which achieves superior performance on several medical benchmarks compared to GPT-4 while using only 40% of the training resources without causing general tasks forgetting. [47] introduce an automated framework that uses large language models to generate domain-specific seed data from corpora like Common Crawl. This seed data guides the mining of relevant documents, improving pre-training quality for domain-specific tasks. Techniques include dataset curation, noise filtering, and advanced augmentation to enhance data diversity and relevance. By enhancing data quality, these data augmentation methods ensure that the model is trained on the most useful and accurate information, enabling models to better generalize and adapt to new tasks and environments. 

C. Process optimization methods 

In process optimization methods, the traditional training process of LLMs is disrupted. A series of papers have proposed new frameworks for continual learning that move beyond the traditional pre-training, fine-tuning, and alignment sequence. These approaches focus on modifying the training process to enhance the model’s learning capabilities, ultimately leading to improved end-task performance. PIT[38] argues that instruction data is more straightforward than raw document data, as documents are often more complex and contain factual content in a more intricate manner. To address this, the study proposes pre-instruction-tuning (PIT), which involves QA instruction fine-tuning prior to continual pre-training, to enhance the model’s ability to extract factual knowledge across various domains. Jindal et al. [39] examines how continual pre-training im-pacts instruction-following ability in LLMs, revealing that it can cause catastrophic forgetting in directly continual pre-training the instruction-tuned models. It also introduces an efficient strategy to gain up-to-date knowledge and instruction-following capabilities in continual learning. [45] integrates instruction tags into the continual pre-training process, preventing loss of conversational proficiency 7

[41] introduces DGA, which uses a proxy KL-divergence loss with different dropout percentages to calculate the impor-tance of different attention heads in the transformer block. It also employs a soft-masking mechanism to preserve important knowledge while adapting to a new domain. Similarly, Ke et al. [51] explores DAS, extending DGA to all neural units, and investigates the adaptation of a sequence of new domain knowledge by incorporating contrastive learning. The Mixture-of-Experts(MoE) architecture [52, 53] has gained increasing popularity for scaling model parameters due to its sparse activation properties. However, training an MoE model from scratch is often constrained by data-hungry and training instability. Zhu et al. [42] conducts LLaMA-MoE, transforming LLaMA2 into MoE architecture and investigat-ing various expert construction methods and data sampling strategies during continual pre-training. IV. C ONTINUAL FINE -TUNING 

Large Language Models have demonstrated exceptional performance in natural language processing and generation tasks. However, with the continuously evolving nature of tasks and data, an important research direction is how to effectively fine-tune these models to adapt to new environments while preserving knowledge of previous tasks. Continual Fine-tuning has been proposed as a strategy to address this challenge.Table II provides an overall summary of continual fine-tuning meth-ods. Continual fine-tuning refers to the process of further ad-justing and optimizing a large model after it has completed pre-training, by introducing new data or tasks. This process significantly differs from traditional fine-tuning methods, as continual fine-tuning emphasizes minimizing the forgetting of existing knowledge while incorporating new information. This approach not only focuses on the model’s performance on new tasks but also on its ability to retain performance on old tasks, thereby achieving a continual accumulation of knowledge. The core aspects of continual fine-tuning include: 

1) Dynamic Adaptability: The model should possess the ability to rapidly adapt to the characteristics of new tasks without the need for retraining from scratch. This dynamic adaptability ensures that the model maintains efficiency and flexibility in diverse task environments. 

2) Forgetting Control: To mitigate or avoid catastrophic forgetting during the fine-tuning process, researchers have employed various strategies and techniques. These measures ensure that the model can effectively retain its performance on previous tasks while learning new ones. 

3) Knowledge Transfer: The transfer and application of existing knowledge across different tasks or domains are crucial for enhancing the model’s learning efficiency and performance. Effective knowledge transfer enables the model to leverage insights gained from prior tasks when learning new ones, thereby improving both efficiency and performance. In the research on continual fine-tuning, many researchers have proposed various methods to effectively achieve dy-namic adaptability, forgetting control, and knowledge trans-fer. Replay-based methods assist models in retaining learned knowledge by preserving data samples from old tasks. Regularization-based methods impose constraints on model parameters to prevent excessive adjustment of existing knowl-edge when learning new tasks. Architecture-based methods explore solutions to continual learning issues by modifying the model architecture. These approaches are not mutually exclusive. With the rapid advancement of continual learning, researchers have introduced numerous efficient learning frame-works that integrate different techniques. 8

A. Replay-based methods 

In the field of continual learning for LLM, replay-based methods are a key strategy designed to address the forgetting problem that models may encounter when learning new tasks. These methods assist models in retaining previously learned knowledge by preserving and utilizing data samples from old tasks, thereby facilitating continual learning. Common strategies include experience replay and generative replay. The former retains a portion of old task data samples during the training of new tasks and mixes them with new data in the training process. This approach enables models to review and reinforce their memory of old knowledge while acquiring new information. [54] conducted the first exploration of continual learning in the context of instruction fine-tuning. They proposed a simple yet effective method that uses a replay mechanism with a certain proportion of prior data. This ap-proach not only helps the model retain performance on earlier tasks but also enhances its generalization ability, enabling it to adapt to new instructions and acquire novel capabilities over time. DYNAINST [55] incorporates an entropy-based dynamic instance selection mechanism and an adaptive task replay strategy. [66] proposes an instruction-based continual learning method for LLMs. It first pre-trains on self-generated negative samples, then fine-tunes on normal data. During CL, it replays past task instructions instead of raw data to prevent forgetting. Instruction-based Continual Learning (InsCL) [68] is a novel replay-based framework for continual instruction tuning. Leveraging task similarity computed via Wasserstein Distance over instructions, InsCL dynamically replays relevant historical data. It further proposes an Instruction Information Metric (InsInfo) to quantify instruction complexity and diver-sity, prioritizing high-quality samples during replay. However, sometimes the original training data may no longer be available due to privacy concerns or access re-strictions. In such situations, some pseudo-sample generation techniques is proposed to create the pseudo-samples that closely resemble the distribution of original data. SAPT [56] incorporates an additional pseudo-sample generator to produce synthetic samples, which helps address catastrophic forgetting. These samples assist the attention reflection module in re-visiting prior knowledge, ensuring the retention of previously learned information. SSR [67] introduces Self-Synthesized Re-hearsal, a novel approach to mitigating catastrophic forgetting in LLMs during continual learning. It leverages the model’s in-context learning capability to generate and iteratively refine synthetic rehearsal data, thereby eliminating the need to store real training samples while achieving superior performance and greater data efficiency compared to traditional rehearsal-based methods. 

B. Regularization-based methods 

Regularization-based continual learning methods represent an effective strategy for mitigating catastrophic forgetting by imposing constraints on model parameter updates. The core idea is to evaluate the importance of individual model parameters to previously learned tasks using regularization terms during the training of new tasks. Based on these evalua-tions, targeted constraints are applied to parameter updates. Specifically, these methods prioritize preserving parameters that are critical for earlier tasks, thereby minimizing significant changes to these parameters during gradient updates. This reduces interference with the retention of prior knowledge during new task training. DYNAINST [55] conducts a novel framework, which com-bines local minima-inducing parameter regularization and dy-namic instruction replay to improve the generalization perfor-mance of PLMs when tasks are presented sequentially. 

C. Architecture-based methods 

Continual learning methods based on model architecture dynamically adjust and expand the network structure to ac-commodate new tasks while preserving and storing knowl-edge from previous tasks. These approaches introduce new modules, network layers, or memory units into the existing model, enabling the model to learn new tasks without losing capabilities on prior tasks. This effectively mitigates the issue of catastrophic forgetting and facilitates knowledge transfer across tasks. A common strategy involves the use of adapters, lightweight network structures typically inserted into existing neural net-work layers as small, trainable modules. When learning a new task, these adapter modules are trained while the pa-rameters of the original model remain frozen. This approach significantly reduces the number of parameters requiring ad-justment, thereby lowering computational and storage costs. Additionally, the modular design of adapters enables both task-specific isolation and cross-task knowledge sharing. By incorporating adapters, models can flexibly transfer knowledge across multiple tasks while maintaining robust memory of previously learned tasks. Multi-LoRA [71] employs multiple LoRA modules for both target and auxiliary tasks, thereby improving the performance of continual information extraction. O-LoRA [57] extends LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) [72] to continual learning by introducing a new LoRA module for each task in a temporal sequence, enabling efficient task-specific adaptation. To mit-igate catastrophic forgetting, O-LoRA leverages the concept of orthogonal subspaces: each LoRA module is treated as an independent subspace, and an orthogonality loss is imposed across modules. This design minimizes interference from new tasks on previously acquired knowledge. By enforcing orthogonality, O-LoRA effectively isolates task representations and alleviates forgetting. In contrast, SAPT [56] contends that orthogonality alone is insufficient to support knowledge transfer across tasks. To address this limitation, SAPT introduces a shared-attention framework that facilitates knowledge fusion. By leveraging attention mechanisms, SAPT enables the exchange of crit-ical information between tasks, thereby enhancing transfer while avoiding the strict knowledge isolation inherent in orthogonality-based methods. As a result, SAPT achieves improved multi-task performance through balanced knowledge sharing and retention. 9

Another approach is Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), which adapts to new tasks by updating only a small subset of trainable parameters. PEFT methods offer the ad-vantage of significantly reducing computational and storage resource requirements while maintaining efficient learning for new tasks. This makes PEFT particularly suitable for resource-constrained scenarios, where it substantially improves training efficiency. Based on the Prompt-tuning [73], [58] proposes a method called progressive prompts, which freezes the model’s parameters and learns a soft prompt for each task, concate-nating it with prompts from previous tasks. Additionally, it introduces a more challenging continual learning task with long sequences. The method achieves the best performance in these settings. Similarly, based on the prefix-tuning[74], [59] explores a continual learning method that eliminates the need for storing historical data and relies on efficient parameter isolation. This approach surpasses all existing methods that do not depend on replay. The method employs dynamic prefix tuning blocks for training and utilizes Mahalanobis distance[75] and Gaussian distributions to identify task types and select the corresponding block for new tasks. MixLoRA[60] proposes a parameter-efficient mixture-of-experts method for fine-tuning large language models. It con-structs multiple LoRA-based experts within the feed-forward network block of a frozen pre-trained dense model and uses a top-k router. It also employs independent attention-layer LoRA adapters and an auxiliary load balance loss to enhance performance and address router imbalance. [61] introduces MIGU, a method for continual learning that does not require rehearsal or task labels. It updates model parameters in linear layers with large magnitudes by leveraging the observation that different tasks have unique magnitude distributions. MIGU demonstrates superior performance on a range of continual learning benchmarks and can be easily integrated with current CL methods to improve overall performance. Diera et al. [62] conducts a DKVB architecture, which allows for localized updates by freezing the model parameters and only updat-ing the discrete key-value pairs. It demonstrates competitive performance with lower computational costs compared to other continual learning methods. The authors also propose a generic discrete key initialization technique that is task-independent, further enhancing the model’s adaptability and efficiency in various NLP tasks. [63] proposes MAC (Mem-ory of Amortized Contexts), an efficient online adaptation framework for LLMs. It freezes the base model and uses an amortization network to compress new data into compact modulations stored in memory. During inference, it retrieves and combines relevant modulations for adaptation—enabling knowledge retention without gradient updates. MAC outper-forms fine-tuning methods in online adaptation tasks. [64] introduces KLDA for class-incremental learning (CIL), using a frozen foundation model’s features enhanced by RBF kernels and Random Fourier Features. It incrementally updates class means and a shared covariance matrix, achieving state-of-the-art performance without replay data—avoiding catastrophic forgetting while matching joint training’s upper bound. [65] conducts CLOB, a verbal-prompt-based continual learning method for black-box LLMs. It introduces CIS, which in-crementally updates class-wise summaries to address context limitations, achieving near-zero forgetting and strong incre-mental performance. [69] explores SwitchCIT, which allevi-ates catastrophic forgetting by using a switch network to route computations to parameter-efficient tuned models. The switch network identifies tasks from instructions and routes them to specialized models fine-tuned via methods like LoRA. [70] conducts L2R, which isolates the training of new PEFT mod-ules to prevent interference with previously learned modules and employs a memory-based router to dynamically compose these modules during inference. This approach enhances gen-eralization and performance by leveraging a small memory of previous examples to learn effective routing functions. In continual learning, the strategy for expanding the network architecture is critical. By dynamically loading, freezing, or extending additional model modules, the system can selec-tively update modules based on the requirements of new tasks without retraining the entire model. This approach effectively avoids catastrophic forgetting while ensuring sufficient adapt-ability for each task. For instance, domain-specific modules can be optimized for particular tasks, while shared modules facilitate cross-task knowledge transfer, enhancing the model’s generalization capabilities. V. C ONTINUAL ALIGNMENT 

Although alignment [76]is a prevalent technique for enhanc-ing the outputs of large language models to better satisfy hu-man preferences, retraining the model to incorporate updating information and evolving preferences presents challenges in computation and efficiency which makes it an impractical solu-tion in real-world applications. To adapt to the evolving social values and preferences, continual alignment has been proposed as a method for updating the model’s output preferences. This includes techniques such as model editing and reinforcement learning etc. A. RL-free methods 

Building on DPO [19], [77] introduced continual optimal policy regularization (COPR), a novel continual alignment approach designed to align language models with evolving human preferences. COPR computes optimal policy distribu-tions and uses them for regularization to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. It also proposed the first TIL and DIL benchmarks for continual value alignment. Wang and Li [78] introduces LEMoE, an advanced Mixture of Experts (MoE) adaptor designed for lifelong model editing. The study presents a novel approach for inserting modules that involves freezing previous expert networks to address issues related to catas-trophic forgetting. Additionally, it introduces a unique KV anchor routing mechanism to improve routing consistency and utilizes clustering-based order planning to optimize the editing sequence. BaFT [79] computes weights for each basis across subspace dimensions based on input representations. This input-dependent weighting mechanism enables BaFT to adaptively manage diverse types of knowledge, achieving a more effective balance between editability and locality. 10 

B. Reinforcement learning methods 

Zhang et al. [80] introduce continual proximal policy op-timization (CPPO) for continual alignment. They build upon PPO [18] and devise a weighting strategy to balance policy learning and knowledge retention. CPPO not only consistently outperforms baseline methods in aligning with human pref-erences but also demonstrates efficient and stable learning in non-continual scenarios. LifeAlign [81] introduces a novel framework for lifelong alignment. It employs a focalized pref-erence optimization strategy that aligns LLMs with emerging preferences while preventing the degradation of knowledge acquired from previous tasks. Furthermore, LifeAlign incor-porates a memory integration mechanism that consolidates de-noised short-term preference representations into stable long-term memory via intrinsic dimensionality reduction, thereby facilitating efficient storage and retrieval of alignment patterns across diverse domains. LANCET [82] introduces a two-stage framework that identifies training data influencing undesirable model outputs and applies Influence function-driven Bregman Optimization (IBO) to refine model behavior. It effectively mitigates inappropriate LLM behaviors, surpasses methods requiring human preference data, and improves interpretability in modeling human preferences. Puthumanaillam et al. [83] introduces the concept of su-peralignment, emphasizing that alignment is a long-term, dynamic process requiring continuous adaptation to evolving human values. Similarly, Elelimy et al. [84] reexamines the foundations of continual reinforcement learning, arguing that traditional MDP-based frameworks—with fixed policies and cumulative rewards—are inadequate for continual learning settings. The paper proposes replacing the MDP formulation with a history process and introduces deviation regret as a new evaluation metric, highlighting the importance of ongoing adaptation and behavioral consistency throughout the learning process rather than focusing solely on the performance of a static final policy. In the field of continual learning in LLMs, benchmarks play a crucial role in evaluating model performance, generalization capabilities, and resistance to catastrophic forgetting. As LLMs are increasingly applied to complex tasks and diverse domains, well-designed benchmarks are essential for assessing their ability to learn and retain knowledge in dynamic environments. Existing benchmarks typically encompass a variety of task types (e.g., classification, generation, reasoning) and simulate real-world task streams to evaluate model performance in incremental learning scenarios. Additionally, benchmarks must consider inter-task relationships, task difficulty distributions, and the diversity of evaluation metrics to comprehensively reflect the strengths and limitations of models in continual learning. This chapter will systematically review and compare mainstream continual learning benchmarks, analyzing their design characteristics, applicable scenarios, and their contri-butions and limitations in evaluating LLMs. In continual pretraining, models are typically trained on specialized vertical domains to enhance their performance in 11 

these specific areas through knowledge injection. Evaluation metrics often involve domain-specific benchmarks. Addition-ally, models are evaluated on general-purpose benchmarks to assess whether catastrophic forgetting has occurred in the original domain. Commonly used general-purpose benchmarks include MMLU and GSM8K, among others. In the continual fine-tuning stage, a series of benchmarks has been proposed, typically consisting of a collection of end-task evaluations. TRACE[85] consists of several distinct chal-lenging tasks, including domain-specific tasks, multilingual capabilities, code generation, and mathematical reasoning. Zhang et al. [86] introduced CITB, a benchmark for Con-tinual Instruction Tuning, addressing the challenge of enabling language models to continuously learn new tasks without forgetting prior knowledge. The authors curated two long task streams, InstrDialog and InstrDialog++, to systematically evaluate various continual learning methods. Their findings highlight the importance of rich natural language instructions and suggests that current continual learning methods fail to fully leverage these instructions. Jang et al. [87] proposed invariantlama, updatedlama, and newlama benchmarks to eval-uate knowledge retention, updating, and acquisition, along with the FUAR metric quantifying learning-forgetting trade-offs. Razdaibiedina et al. [58] introduce a long sequence benchmark that consists of 15 text classification tasks, which has been widely adopted. Wu et al. [88] conducts Stream-Bench, a benchmark for assessing LLM agents’ continu-ous improvement via input-feedback sequences. It introduces cost-effective, multi-agent baselines that enhance performance through shared memory, advancing adaptive AI in streaming scenarios. VIII. C HALLENGES AND OPPORTUNITIES 

A. Challanges 

Despite notable progress, continual learning still faces two fundamental challenges: catastrophic forgetting and limited knowledge transfer. These issues hinder the ability of models to accumulate and reuse knowledge effectively across tasks. Future research can therefore advance in two primary direc-tions. (1) Improving performance through robust and efficient learning mechanisms. A key objective is to develop methods that can completely eliminate forgetting while accelerating knowledge transfer between tasks. Such advancements would enable CL models to achieve stable and scalable lifelong adaptation. (2) Advancing online continual learning and real-world applications. Bringing CL into dynamic, real-world environments requires models capable of online adaptation under limited supervision and data constraints. Within replay-based paradigms, pseudo-sample generation has emerged as a promising approach to mitigate data privacy concerns. To make this strategy more effective, future work should focus on improving both the quality and diversity of pseudo-samples while maintaining distributional alignment with the original data. For instance, [89] proposes an LLM-driven framework, where a teacher LLM synthesizes task-specific data to augment small seed datasets for fine-tuning. This approach illustrates the potential of leveraging generative models for privacy-preserving data expansion. Furthermore, techniques from federated learning [90], including distributed data processing and differential privacy, could be integrated to further protect historical data during replay. 

B. Opportunities 

Despite the recent progress in continual learning for large language models (LLMs), many promising research opportu-nities remain open. Potential directions include, but are not limited to, the following: 

1) Improving Performance: Developing more effective methods to enhance the efficiency, scalability, and robustness of continual learning remains a central challenge. From a theoretical perspective, several studies have attempted to un-derstand and address catastrophic forgetting through principled geometric and representational analyses. For example, ProNC [91] extends the Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF) structure as class prototypes in a task-incremental manner, preserving the geometric separability between old and new classes while minimizing feature drift of previously learned categories. 

2) Multimodal Continual Learning: Current research in continual learning (CL) has predominantly focused on lan-guage models within the NLP domain, while multimodal continual learning (MM-CL) remains relatively underexplored. Extending continual learning to multimodal scenarios (e.g., vi-sion–language or speech–language tasks) opens new opportu-nities for building models that can adapt across heterogeneous data modalities. Unlike unimodal settings, multimodal con-tinual learning must address not only catastrophic forgetting within each modality but also cross-modal alignment drift, where representations between modalities gradually become inconsistent over time. Recent work by [92, 93, 94, 95] proposes efficient continual instruction-tuning frameworks for multimodal models, enabling them to incrementally acquire new multimodal capabilities with reduced forgetting and computational costs. Future research could further explore modality-invariant representations, adaptive prompt mecha-nisms, and cross-modal replay strategies to enhance the scala-bility and robustness of multimodal continual learning systems in complex, real-world environments. 

3) Reinforcement Learning Approaches: Recent studies suggest that LLMs trained through reinforcement learning can retain acquired knowledge more effectively, indicating the potential of RL-based strategies for advancing continual learning. Leveraging RL in CL enables models to actively interact with the learning environment, facilitating adaptive knowledge acquisition and long-term retention. [96] provides a comprehensive survey of RL methods in the context of continual learning, highlighting their effectiveness in balancing knowledge retention and exploration. For example, [97, 98] employs reinforcement learning to enhance knowledge trans-fer. These approaches demonstrate that integrating RL with CL can improve adaptive learning, policy generalization, and resilience to catastrophic forgetting, offering a promising di-rection for future research. 

4) Semi-Parametric Methods: Most traditional continual learning approaches, including those discussed earlier, rely on 12 

fully parametric models, which continuously update model parameters to incorporate new knowledge. Recently, semi-parametric methods have emerged as a promising alternative [99, 100]. These approaches combine parametric model up-dates with non-parametric memory mechanisms, aiming to achieve a more effective balance between plasticity (the ability to learn new knowledge) and stability (the preservation of prior knowledge). Semi-parametric continual learning frameworks often employ external memory modules, retrieval-augmented components, or agent-based architectures that selectively recall relevant past information without overwriting model parame-ters. By decoupling memory storage from parameter updates, these strategies enable lifelong learning with improved knowl-edge retention and adaptability. 

5) Online Continual Learning: In real-world environments, task boundaries are often ambiguous or non-existent, making it impractical to assume clearly segmented learning phases. Consequently, designing algorithms capable of learning from streaming data in real time while mitigating catastrophic forgetting has become a key research frontier for the practical deployment of continual learning systems. Recent studies [101, 102] have explored online continual learning (OCL) frameworks that continuously update models as new data arrives, without explicitly revisiting past tasks. IX. C ONCLUSION 

Although large language models demonstrate powerful per-formance, their internal parameters and knowledge remain static. Continual learning for large language models enables them to incrementally acquire new knowledge and update their parameters. This survey reviews recent advancements in continual learning for large language models, categorizing research into three stages of model training: pretraining, fine-tuning, and alignment. For each stage, we classify and discuss the studies based on their methodologies. Additionally, we summarize relevant benchmark studies, key challenges, and future directions in this field. We hope that this survey can contribute to the development of continual learning for large language models. REFERENCES
