# Abstract 

Multimodal reasoning in vision-language models (VLMs) typically relies on a two-stage process: su-pervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learn-ing (RL). In standard SFT, all tokens contribute equally to the loss, even though reasoning data are inherently token-imbalanced. Long <think> traces overshadow short but task-critical <answer> seg-ments, leading to verbose reasoning and inaccu-rate answers. We propose a SCALe – Sched-uled Curriculum Adaptive Loss that explic-itly separates supervision over reasoning and an-swer segments using dynamic, length-independent weighting. Unlike vanilla SFT, which overweights the <think> segment, SCALe-SFT gradually shifts the focus from <think> to <answer> throughout training via a cosine scheduling policy, encourag-ing concise and well-grounded reasoning. We eval-uate SCALe across diverse benchmarks and archi-tectures. Results show that SCALe consistently im-proves accuracy over vanilla SFT and matches the performance of the full two-phase SFT + GRPO pipeline while requiring only about one-seventh of the training time, making it a lightweight yet ef-fective alternative. When combined with GRPO, SCALe achieves the best overall performance, high-lighting its value both as a standalone method and as a strong foundation for reinforcement refinement.

# 2. Related Work 

Advances in Reasoning in VLMs Recent vision-language models (VLMs) have moved beyond single-step perception to reasoning-centric genera-tion, producing intermediate chains that connect visual evidence with textual conclusions [1, 4]. Modern VLMs typically follow a two-stage train-ing paradigm: supervised fine-tuning on reasoning-annotated data to establish structured reasoning priors, followed by reinforcement learning alignment with task-specific rewards [2, 3, 5, 8]. This combi-nation of structured reasoning and reward-driven optimization has improved performance and inter-pretability across domains such as medical imaging [13], video understanding [7, 14], and agentic GUI control [6, 15], enhancing the safety and reliability of multimodal reasoning systems [16, 17]. 

Reward-based Reasoning Guidance Recent work increasingly focuses on guiding large models’ reasoning during training for greater accuracy and efficiency. Most advances occur at the RL stage, using improved reward functions or optimized al-gorithms [18–25]. A growing emphasis is placed on shaping the structure and coherence of reason-ing rather than merely its surface characteristics. Process-level reward functions evaluate intermedi-ate reasoning steps to encourage coherent thought progression [22, 23], whereas verifier-based rewards (RLVR) replace human preference models with pro-grammatic or verifiable signals to evaluate the cor-rectness of reasoning traces [24, 25]. At the algorith-mic level, RL framewords like GRPO has emerged as the dominant paradigm for reasoning-oriented RL. First introduced in DeepSeekMath [26] and later refined in DeepSeek-R1 [2], GRPO compares sets of sampled reasoning paths, rewarding those that outperform others in relative terms. This ap-proach improves both exploration efficiency and re-ward utilization. Overall, Recent progress centers on reward shaping and RL optimization, while the SFT stage, where models first learn coherent rea-soning remains underexplored. 

# 3. Methods 

We follow the standard Vision-R1 two-phase train-ing pipeline comprising (1) supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and (2) reinforcement learning 

with GRPO. Our work focuses on the SFT stage, where we replace the standard cross-entropy with a scheduled weighted loss: A dynamic weighting strategy that provides a useful inductive bias that stabilizes optimization by balancing the generation of reasoning with its alignment to the final answer. This design enables a VLM to produce coherent rea-soning traces and accurate final answers. 

Reasoning Dataset Recent reasoning-focused VLM research relies on datasets that explicitly cou-ple visual understanding with structured reason-ing traces. These datasets extend beyond simple question-answer pairs by including stepwise thought processes annotations. Each sample contains an im-age, a textual question, and a reasoning trace de-limited by <think>...</think> followed by a final answer enclosed in <answer>...</answer> . No-table datasets include Mulberry [27] and Visual-CoT [28]. Most are composed from diverse vi-sual tasks such as captioning, VQA, and chart-understanding datasets, reformatted or synthesized for reasoning-oriented supervision. These datasets are used in the SFT stage to instill reasoning ability in models that lack structured thinking.

In such tasks, the model often generates long and verbose reasoning traces enclosed within <think>...</think> , yet evaluation metrics depend primarily on the final short answer segment (<answer>...</answer> ). As a result, the vanilla CE objective may overem-phasize reasoning fluency at the expense of fac-tual correctness, causing the model to “get lost in thought”, producing extended reasoning that fails to yield accurate answers. In such cases, the wrong answer can be effectively drowned out by the “think-ing” loss, with this discrepancy barely reflected in the loss signal. To address this limitation, we introduce aweighted loss that explicitly separates reasoning and answer supervision. Each output sequence is di-vided into two semantic segments, the reasoning span enclosed by <think> tags and the answer span enclosed by <answer> tags—allowing each segment to be weighted and optimized independently. 3.2. GRPO Reinforcement Refinement 

Following the SFT stage, an additional GRPO phase can be introduced to further refine reason-ing structure and factual correctness, although this stage is not mandatory for the overall training pipeline. The total reward combines two comple-mentary signals: 1. Format-structure reward rtag , which mea-sures the correctness of the reasoning–answer tag placement. Each generated sample is evaluated on four key locations: the opening <think> , the closing </think> , the opening <answer> , and the closing </answer> tag. A partial score of 0.25 is awarded for each correctly placed tag, yielding: 

rtag = 0 .25 × ncorrect , (5) where ncorrect ∈ { 0, 1, 2, 3, 4} denotes the num-ber of correctly located tags in the output se-quence. This structured reward encourages the model to maintain proper reasoning boundaries and to consistently terminate its thought process before generating the final answer. 2. Answer-accuracy reward rans , which evalu-ates an exact match between the generated and reference answers. The final reward is a weighted combination: 

r = λtag rtag + λans rans . (6) This two-part signal encourages the model to main-tain coherent reasoning structure while improv-ing factual correctness. Initialization from the weighted-loss checkpoint provides a stable transi-tion into reinforcement optimization, leading to im-proved reasoning consistency and alignment. Our two-stage training, scheduled weighted SFT followed by GRPO, jointly shapes how the model reasons and what it answers. The length-independent weighted loss promotes interpretable, balanced reasoning during supervised learning, while GRPO fine-tunes reasoning faithfulness and answer accuracy through reinforcement feedback. 

# 4. Experiments 

4.1. Experimental Setup 

We compare five configurations: (1) Baseline model before fine tuning (Base), (2) SFT, (3) SCALe-SFT (ours), (4) SFT-GRPO initialized from the SFT checkpoint, and (5) SCALe-GRPO initialized from weighted-loss (SCALe) SFT. 

Models. We evaluate our proposed weighted loss and GRPO training pipeline on three open-source vision–language models: Qwen2.5-VL-3B [29], which has undergone prior reasoning-oriented train-ing, and two non-reasoning-pretrained models in-cluding LLaVA-Next [30] and Gemma-3-4B [31]. This setup allows us to examine the adaptability of SCALe-SFT across both reasoning-oriented models and those without existing reasoning priors. 

Datasets. We used data from the Vision-R1 rea-soning corpus for both SFT and GRPO training. Vision-R1 [8] is a large-scale multimodal reason-ing dataset with 200K samples. It is generated via modality bridging pipeline using MLLMs out-puts that are in turn fed to DeepSeek-R1 [2], yield-ing structured CoT traces without human anno-tation. We evaluate our approach using LMMS-Eval library [32] on two reasoning-oriented multi-modal benchmarks extracted from Vision-R1: Sci-enceQA [33] and IconQA [34]. ScienceQA con-tains image-based and text-only science questions covering natural, social, and language sciences, each paired with multiple-choice answers (A–D) and con-cise human-written explanations. The task requires both visual understanding and scientific reasoning. IconQA focuses on diagram and chart comprehen-sion. Together, these datasets assess both factual reasoning and visual grounding capabilities of vi-sion–language models. The training set for science related samples from Vision-R1, consisting of ScienceQA and SQA [27] has 6 , 593 samples and for IconQA 15 , 241 samples. The test set consists of 2 , 017 samples from Sci-enceQA. IconQA test set includes three question types: fill-in-the-blank , choose-text , and choose-image . Since our models were trained on single-image reasoning, results are reported only for the first two categories and consists of 9 , 954 samples.

4.3. Ablation Study 

To isolate the contribution of the scheduling mech-anism in our weighted loss, we conduct an abla-tion on the Qwen2.5-VL-3B model using both the ScienceQA and IconQA benchmarks. In this ex-periment, we compare our SCALe-SFT configura-tion, where the reasoning weight decays over train-ing, with a control variant in which the schedule is effectively neutral, starting and ending at identical weights of 1 .0. This variant is denoted as FW (fixed weights) and represents a case where scheduling has no influence on token weighting. We evaluate both the SFT and GRPO stages under these two setups. Table 3 shows that incorporating a scheduled de-cay of the reasoning weight provides consistent gains across both datasets. The SFT-FW variant, is still better than vanilla SFT, but the full SCALe-SFT has a relative improved average of 0.45%. The ab-sence of scheduling also limits the benefit of GRPO. While GRPO-FW yields an improvement over SFT, the gain remains smaller than with full SCALe-GRPO, which has an average improvment of 1.92%. Without a progressive decay of the reasoning weight, the SFT stage fails to establish a clear bal-ance between reasoning and answer supervision, re-sulting in reasoning traces that are noisier or less distinctly segmented. Consequently, during GRPO training—where rewards are concentrated around tag placement and answer accuracy—the model re-ceives high-variance reinforcement signals that can over-correct or truncate its reasoning, leading to marginal degradation. In contrast, the scheduled SCALe pretraining provides a smoother structural prior that aligns naturally with GRPO rewards, en-abling stable optimization and consistent gains. These results confirm that the gradual schedul-ing of reasoning weight acts as a lightweight curricu-lum mechanism, guiding the model to first focus on structured reasoning and later refine answer preci-sion.

# 5. Conclusion 

We introduced a SCALe-SFT – Scheduled Cur-riculum Adaptive Loss SFT for vision–language rea-soning, a simple modification to supervised fine-tuning that applies dynamic, length-independent weighting over reasoning and answer spans. The proposed SCALe-SFT encourages concise and well-structured reasoning, leading to more ac-curate final answers. It serves both as a lightweight alternative to reinforcement learning and as astronger initialization for subsequent GRPO opti-mization using tag-location and answer-accuracy re-wards, resulting in improved reasoning alignment. Experiments show consistent gains over vanilla SFT and GRPO alone. Termination analysis shows SCALe-SFT sharply reduces invalid generations (unclosed <answer> ), thereby reducing the need for repeated inference to recover valid outputs. Abla-tion study indicates that replacing the cosine sched-ule with fixed weights reduced performance gains, confirming that dynamic weighting is better for ef-fective reasoning supervision. Future work will extend the proposed framework to a broader range of and modalities and multi-image cases. Another research direction will be to explore an automatic online weight tuning. We also plan to apply our method to language-only reason-ing datasets. Since SCALe-SFT operates purely on token-level supervision, it can be directly applied to textual reasoning tasks without modification, pro-viding a unified approach to structured reasoning across visual and linguistic domains. Such exten-sions will help assess whether the benefits observed here, improved reasoning structure, reduced invalid terminations, and enhanced answer alignment, gen-eralize beyond visual question answering to broader reasoning scenarios. # B. Additional Qualitative Results 

Figures 4 - 5 present additional examples comparing the reasoning structures produced by the different STF variations from the models compared in the main paper. All examples are taken from HF test set lmms-lab/ScienceQA-IMG dataset. Blue bot-tom right box is reasoning trace from vanilla-SFT and orange bottom left shows SCALe-SFT reason-ing. Our SCALe model demonstrates more coherent intermediate reasoning steps, encourages the model to complete its reasoning trace coherently and con-verge toward the correct conclusion, resulting in more reliable <think> endings and answer transi-tions compared to standard SFT. This can be seen by fig 4, where vanilla SFT goes into a reasoning loop, where it endlessly debates about the answer and fails to reach a final conclusion. In contrast, fig 5 shows the vanilla SFT often can produce co-herent reasoning trace but incorrect final answer, reflecting the token imbalance that biases optimiza-tion toward verbose reasoning segments. 

# C. Implementation Details 

Training. All models were trained using 

Deepspeed-ZeRO2 on 8 ×H100 GPUs. SFT training ran for one epoch over the respective Vision-R1 subsets. We used mixed precision (bfloat16 )and gradient checkpointing. All models were fine-tuned using LoRA adapters with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4 and a maximum sequence length of 8192 tokens. For the RL stage, we employed GRPO with a maximum generation length of 1024 tokens, generating 8 completions per prompt using sampling with temperature 1 .0 (top-p = 1 .0, no top-k filtering). Inference was accelerated using vLLM in colocate mode. 

Evaluation. We evaluated all models using the 

lmms-eval framework. To accommodate reasoning-based responses, we extended lmms-eval to parse the final answer from within the <answer> tags. All metrics were calculated exclusively on the extracted final answers. We used greedy decoding (tempera-ture 0) for all evaluations to ensure deterministic and reproducible results.
